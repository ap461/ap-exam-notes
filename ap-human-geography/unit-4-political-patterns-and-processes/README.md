## What is Unit 4 about?

Unit 4 of AP Human Geography studies how political power is organized across space — how states form, how borders are drawn, how governments are structured, and how forces unite or divide countries. You'll learn the difference between a state, a nation, and a nation-state, the types of boundaries and how they create conflict, what causes devolution, and how supranational organizations like the EU and UN reshape sovereignty. This unit makes up about 12–17% of the exam and is heavy on case studies — expect to apply concepts to specific countries like Sudan, Belgium, Spain, or Ukraine.

## Key concepts to master

### State, nation, and nation-state

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **State** | A politically organized territory with sovereignty, defined borders, and a permanent population | France, Japan, Brazil |
| **Nation** | A group of people with shared culture, language, history, or identity | Kurds, Palestinians, Basques |
| **Nation-state** | A state where most citizens share one national identity | Japan, Iceland, Portugal |
| **Stateless nation** | A nation without its own state | Kurds, Palestinians, Roma |
| **Multinational state** | A state with multiple nations inside it | Russia, Canada, Belgium |
| **Multistate nation** | A nation spread across multiple states | Koreans (North + South Korea) |
| **Autonomous region** | A defined area within a state with some self-government | Catalonia, Hong Kong, Scotland |

### Sovereignty and self-determination

- **Sovereignty** — a state's authority to govern itself without outside interference
- **Self-determination** — the right of a nation to choose its own political status
- **Territoriality** — a group's connection to and defense of a specific territory
- **De jure** — by law (officially recognized)
- **De facto** — in practice (operating without formal recognition)

### Boundaries (super important)

| Boundary type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Antecedent** | Drawn before settlement | U.S.–Canada border (49th parallel) |
| **Subsequent** | Drawn after cultural patterns formed | Northern Ireland / Republic of Ireland |
| **Superimposed** | Forced by outsiders, ignoring local culture | Africa's colonial borders |
| **Relict** | No longer functions but still visible | Berlin Wall, Hadrian's Wall |
| **Geometric** | Straight lines, latitude/longitude | U.S.–Canada west of Lake of the Woods |
| **Physical / natural** | Follows rivers, mountains, oceans | Rio Grande (U.S.–Mexico) |
| **Cultural / ethnic** | Follows cultural divisions | India–Pakistan partition |

**Boundary processes:** definition (legal description) → delimitation (drawn on map) → demarcation (physically marked on the ground) → administration (enforced day-to-day).

### Forces that hold or break states apart

| Force | What it does | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Centripetal** | Unites people, holds state together | Shared language, common enemy, strong national identity |
| **Centrifugal** | Divides people, threatens state unity | Ethnic conflict, religious divisions, economic inequality |
| **Devolution** | Transfer of power from central government to regions | Scotland (UK), Catalonia (Spain), Quebec (Canada) |
| **Balkanization** | Fragmentation of a state into smaller hostile units | Former Yugoslavia |
| **Irredentism** | A state claims territory in another state where its nation lives | Russia/Ukraine, Nazi Germany/Sudetenland |

### Forms of government

- **Unitary state** — power concentrated in central government (France, Japan, China)
- **Federal state** — power shared between central and regional governments (U.S., Germany, India)
- **Confederation** — loose alliance of independent states (rare today; EU is partially confederal)

### Electoral geography

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Reapportionment** | Redistributing legislative seats based on new census data |
| **Redistricting** | Redrawing district boundaries within a state |
| **Gerrymandering** | Drawing districts to favor a political party or group |
| **Cracking** | Splitting a voting bloc across many districts to dilute power |
| **Packing** | Concentrating a voting bloc in one district to limit its overall influence |
| **Majority-minority district** | A district where a minority group is the majority of voters |

### Supranationalism

- **Supranational organization** — alliance of states pooling sovereignty (EU, UN, NATO, ASEAN, USMCA)
- **Trade-off** — gain economic and security benefits, lose some sovereignty
- **Brexit** — UK leaving EU = pushback against supranationalism

## Geopolitical theories

### Heartland Theory (Mackinder)
Whoever controls Eastern Europe controls the "Heartland" of Eurasia. Whoever controls the Heartland controls the World Island. Whoever controls the World Island controls the world. Used to justify Cold War containment.

### Rimland Theory (Spykman)
Counter to Mackinder — the coastal "Rimland" surrounding the Heartland is the real key to global power. Influenced U.S. Cold War strategy of building alliances along the Eurasian coast (NATO, Japan, Korea).

### Organic Theory (Ratzel)
States are like living organisms — they need to grow to survive. Used (and misused) to justify imperialism and Nazi expansion.

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing state with nation** — state = political entity; nation = cultural group
- **Mixing up centripetal and centrifugal** — centripetal pulls together; centrifugal pulls apart
- **Confusing antecedent with subsequent boundaries** — antecedent comes BEFORE settlement; subsequent comes AFTER
- **Treating "country" as the AP term** — the AP exam uses "state" almost exclusively
- **Forgetting devolution can be peaceful** — Scotland's devolution is legal; Yugoslavia's was violent
- **Mixing up cracking and packing** — cracking splits a group; packing concentrates them

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 4?**
Unit 4 makes up about 12–17% of multiple-choice questions and is one of the most common FRQ units. Expect at least one question about boundaries, devolution, or centripetal/centrifugal forces.

**What's the difference between a state and a country?**
On the AP HUG exam, "state" and "country" mean the same thing — a politically organized territory with sovereignty. The AP exam almost always uses "state." Don't confuse this with U.S. states like Texas or Ohio, which are sub-units of a federal state.

**Why is Africa's colonial border situation important?**
Africa's borders are mostly **superimposed** — drawn by European colonizers at the 1884–85 Berlin Conference without regard for ethnic or cultural groups. This split many nations across multiple states (Tutsi in Rwanda/Burundi/DRC) and forced rival groups inside the same state, fueling ethnic conflict for over a century.

**Is the EU a state?**
No. The EU is a **supranational organization** — a body of states that pool some sovereignty for shared economic and political goals. EU members remain independent states with their own governments, militaries, and citizenship.

**What's the difference between gerrymandering and redistricting?**
Redistricting is the routine process of redrawing electoral district boundaries (usually after a census). Gerrymandering is when redistricting is done unfairly to favor a party or group, typically through cracking (splitting opposing voters) or packing (concentrating them).

**Are stateless nations a small problem?**
No. Stateless nations include groups of tens of millions of people. The Kurds (~30 million across Turkey, Iraq, Iran, Syria) are the largest stateless nation, followed by groups like the Palestinians, the Roma, and the Tibetans.
