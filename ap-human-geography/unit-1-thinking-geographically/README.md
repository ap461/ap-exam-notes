## What is Unit 1 about?

Unit 1 of AP Human Geography teaches you to think like a geographer. You learn how to read different types of maps, understand the distortions in map projections, work with scale, identify regions, and use geographic data to answer questions about the world. This is the smallest unit by exam weight (8–10%), but every later unit relies on these tools — you can't analyze population, agriculture, or cities without first understanding how to map them.

## Key concepts to master

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Absolute vs relative location** | Coordinates vs description ("30°N, 90°W" vs "near the river") |
| **Distance decay** | Interaction between two places decreases as distance increases |
| **Time-space compression** | Modern transport and tech make distant places feel closer |
| **Scale** | Map scale (cartographic) vs scale of analysis (local, regional, global) |
| **Region types** | Formal (uniform), functional (nodal), perceptual (vernacular) |
| **Geographic data sources** | GIS, GPS, remote sensing, census, field observation |
| **Spatial patterns** | Clustered, dispersed, random — describing how things are distributed |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Mixing up choropleth and dot maps** — choropleth uses shaded regions, dot uses individual points
- **Confusing Mercator and Peters** — Mercator preserves shape (distorts area near poles), Peters preserves area (distorts shape)
- **Forgetting that scale works two ways** — large scale = small area zoomed in, small scale = large area zoomed out
- **Treating regions as fixed** — perceptual regions (like "the South" or "the Midwest") are subjective and change over time

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 1?**
Unit 1 is the smallest by exam weight — about 8–10% of multiple-choice questions. However, the tools from this unit (maps, scale, regions) appear across nearly every other unit's questions, so it's foundational even if it's small on its own.

**Do I need to memorize all map projections?**
You should know Mercator, Peters, Robinson, and Goode's homolosine, plus what each preserves and distorts. The exam often shows a projection and asks why a cartographer chose it.

**What's the difference between formal, functional, and perceptual regions?**
A formal region has a shared trait (language, climate). A functional region is organized around a node (a city's metro area). A perceptual region exists in people's minds (the South, the Bible Belt) and isn't officially defined.

**Is this the easiest AP HUG unit?**
Many students find Unit 1 the most abstract because it's about thinking tools rather than concrete content. Once you've finished Units 2–7, Unit 1 often makes more sense in hindsight.
