## What is Unit 3 about?

Unit 3 of AP Human Geography studies how culture is created, spread, and transformed across space. You'll learn the different types of cultural diffusion (relocation, expansion, hierarchical, contagious, stimulus), the major language families and world religions, and how cultural landscapes reflect the values of the people who shape them. This unit is heavy on vocabulary and case studies — about 13–15% of the exam — and shows up frequently in FRQs about diffusion patterns and globalization.

## Key concepts to master

### Culture basics

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Culture** | Shared beliefs, values, practices, and material objects of a group |
| **Cultural trait** | A single attribute (a food, a phrase, a custom) |
| **Cultural complex** | A group of traits that fit together (e.g., a religion's practices) |
| **Material culture** | Physical objects (clothing, buildings, tools) |
| **Non-material culture** | Beliefs, language, customs, traditions |
| **Folk culture** | Small, traditional, place-based, slow to change |
| **Popular culture** | Large-scale, fast-changing, spread by mass media |
| **Cultural landscape** | The visible imprint of culture on the land |

### Cultural diffusion (the big one)

| Type | How it spreads |
|---|---|
| **Relocation diffusion** | People physically move and bring culture with them (immigration) |
| **Expansion diffusion** | Culture spreads outward while staying strong at the source |
| **Hierarchical diffusion** | Spreads from powerful/large places to smaller ones (fashion trends, hip-hop) |
| **Contagious diffusion** | Spreads rapidly to everyone nearby (viral memes, disease) |
| **Stimulus diffusion** | An idea spreads but is modified along the way (McDonald's veggie menu in India) |
| **Reverse hierarchical** | Spreads from smaller places upward (Walmart spread from rural to urban) |

### Language

- **Language family** — group of languages with a common ancestor (Indo-European, Sino-Tibetan, Afro-Asiatic)
- **Language branch** — subdivision within a family (Germanic, Romance within Indo-European)
- **Lingua franca** — common language used between speakers of different native languages (English globally)
- **Pidgin** — simplified mix of two languages used for trade or contact
- **Creole** — pidgin that becomes a community's first language
- **Dialect** — regional variation in vocabulary, grammar, or pronunciation
- **Isogloss** — boundary line where one dialect ends and another begins
- **Toponyms** — place names that reveal cultural history

### World religions

| Type | Examples | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Universalizing** | Christianity, Islam, Buddhism | Seek converts, spread globally |
| **Ethnic** | Hinduism, Judaism | Tied to a specific ethnic group, don't actively recruit |
| **Animism / Indigenous** | Shinto, traditional African religions | Tied to place and ancestors |

**Key religious concepts:**
- **Hearth** — the place where a religion originated
- **Pilgrimage** — religious journey to a sacred site
- **Sacred sites** — Mecca, Jerusalem, Bodh Gaya, Vatican City
- **Sharia, halal, kosher** — religious laws governing behavior and food

### Ethnicity, race, and identity

- **Ethnicity** — shared cultural heritage (language, religion, ancestry)
- **Race** — socially constructed category, often based on physical traits
- **Nationality** — legal membership in a state
- **Gender roles** — how cultures define expected behavior by gender
- **Centripetal forces** — unite people (shared language, religion, history)
- **Centrifugal forces** — divide people (ethnic conflict, religious tension, language differences)

## Cultural change processes

| Process | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Acculturation** | Adopting some traits of another culture while keeping your own |
| **Assimilation** | Fully blending into a dominant culture, losing original traits |
| **Syncretism** | Two cultures blend to create something new (Day of the Dead = Catholic + Aztec) |
| **Cultural appropriation** | Taking elements from a culture without understanding or respecting them |
| **Cultural convergence** | Cultures becoming more similar through contact (globalization) |
| **Cultural divergence** | Cultures becoming more distinct (often as resistance to globalization) |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Mixing up types of diffusion** — hierarchical (top-down) vs contagious (everyone nearby) vs stimulus (modified)
- **Confusing relocation diffusion with expansion diffusion** — relocation requires physical movement; expansion doesn't
- **Treating folk and popular culture as opposites** — they exist on a spectrum, not as binaries
- **Mixing up universalizing and ethnic religions** — universalizing seeks converts; ethnic doesn't
- **Confusing acculturation and assimilation** — acculturation keeps original culture; assimilation loses it
- **Calling all language differences "dialects"** — true dialects are mutually intelligible variations of one language

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 3?**
Unit 3 makes up about 13–15% of the multiple-choice exam. It also appears frequently in FRQs, especially questions about diffusion patterns and the impact of globalization.

**What's the difference between hierarchical and contagious diffusion?**
Hierarchical diffusion spreads from large or powerful places to smaller ones — fashion trends starting in Paris and reaching small towns. Contagious diffusion spreads rapidly to everyone in close proximity, regardless of status — like a viral video or a disease outbreak.

**Are Christianity and Islam the only universalizing religions?**
The three universalizing religions on the AP exam are Christianity, Islam, and Buddhism. They actively seek converts and have spread globally beyond their hearths.

**What's the difference between assimilation and acculturation?**
Acculturation is when a group adopts some traits of a dominant culture but keeps its original culture (a Mexican family in the U.S. speaks English at school but Spanish at home). Assimilation is when a group fully blends into the dominant culture and loses its original identity over time.

**Is hip-hop an example of hierarchical or contagious diffusion?**
Hip-hop spread through hierarchical diffusion — it started in major cities like New York and Los Angeles and spread outward to smaller cities and globally through media and music labels.
