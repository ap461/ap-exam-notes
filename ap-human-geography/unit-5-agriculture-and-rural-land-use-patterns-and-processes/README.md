## What is Unit 5 about?

Unit 5 of AP Human Geography studies how humans produce food and shape rural landscapes. You'll learn the three agricultural revolutions, the difference between subsistence and commercial farming, the von Thünen model of agricultural land use, the Green Revolution and its trade-offs, and how globalization affects modern food systems. This unit makes up about 12–17% of the exam and is heavy on case studies — expect to apply concepts to specific regions like the U.S. Midwest, sub-Saharan Africa, or Southeast Asia.

## Key concepts to master

### The three agricultural revolutions

| Revolution | When | What changed |
|---|---|---|
| **First (Neolithic)** | ~10,000 BCE | Shift from hunting/gathering to settled farming and animal domestication |
| **Second** | 1700s–1800s | Mechanization, crop rotation, selective breeding — supported the Industrial Revolution |
| **Third (Green Revolution)** | 1950s–1970s | High-yield seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, irrigation — boosted yields in developing world |

### Agricultural hearths (origins)

- **Southwest Asia (Fertile Crescent)** — wheat, barley, sheep, goats
- **East Asia (China)** — rice, soybeans, pigs
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** — sorghum, yams, coffee
- **Mesoamerica** — maize (corn), beans, squash
- **Andean South America** — potatoes, quinoa, llamas
- **Southeast Asia** — rice, taro, bananas

### Subsistence vs commercial agriculture

| Feature | Subsistence | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Feed the family | Sell for profit |
| **Scale** | Small farms | Large farms |
| **Labor** | Family labor | Hired or mechanized |
| **Where** | Developing world (Africa, South Asia, parts of Latin America) | Developed world (U.S., Canada, Europe, Australia) |
| **Examples** | Shifting cultivation, intensive subsistence, pastoral nomadism | Mixed crop/livestock, dairying, plantation, ranching |

### Types of agriculture

| Type | Where | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Shifting cultivation (slash-and-burn)** | Tropics — Amazon, Congo, Southeast Asia | Clear forest, farm a few years, move on |
| **Intensive subsistence** | South/East Asia | Heavy labor on small plots, especially wet rice |
| **Pastoral nomadism** | Dry regions — Sahara, Central Asia, Middle East | Herding animals across grasslands |
| **Plantation** | Tropics — bananas, coffee, sugar, rubber | Large estates growing one cash crop for export |
| **Mixed crop and livestock** | U.S. Midwest, Europe | Crops feed animals; both sold |
| **Dairy farming** | Near urban areas (milkshed) | Milk production for nearby cities |
| **Mediterranean** | Mediterranean climates — Italy, California, Chile | Olives, grapes, citrus |
| **Commercial gardening (truck farming)** | U.S. Southeast, California | Fruits and vegetables for distant markets |
| **Ranching** | Dry grasslands — U.S. West, Argentina, Australia | Large-scale cattle or sheep grazing |

### Land survey patterns

- **Metes and bounds** — uses natural landmarks (trees, rivers); irregular shapes (eastern U.S.)
- **Township and range** — grid system based on lat/long (most of central/western U.S.)
- **Long-lot** — narrow strips perpendicular to a river or road (French Canada, Louisiana)

### Rural settlement patterns

- **Clustered (nucleated)** — homes grouped together, fields outside (traditional Europe)
- **Dispersed** — isolated farms spread across the landscape (U.S. Midwest)
- **Linear** — settlements along a road, river, or coast

## Von Thünen Model (must know!)

A theoretical model of how agricultural land use is organized around a central market. Closer to market = higher land cost = more intensive use.

| Ring | Land use | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **1 (closest)** | Dairy and market gardening | Perishable, needs quick transport |
| **2** | Forest (firewood, lumber) | Heavy, costly to transport long distances |
| **3** | Field crops (grains) | Less perishable, easier to ship |
| **4 (farthest)** | Ranching / livestock | Animals can walk to market |

**Key takeaway:** the model assumes a flat, uniform plain with one market. Real-world land use is shaped by climate, technology, and transport routes — but the von Thünen logic still applies (e.g., dairies near cities, ranching in remote areas).

## The Green Revolution

A 1950s–70s program that introduced high-yielding seeds, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation to developing countries (especially India, Mexico, Philippines).

| Benefits | Costs |
|---|---|
| Massive increase in food production | Heavy reliance on chemicals, fertilizers, water |
| Helped India and Mexico become food-secure | Soil degradation, water pollution |
| Reduced famine in many regions | Wealthy farmers benefited more than poor |
| Boosted average yields globally | Loss of traditional crop varieties (genetic diversity) |
| | Did not reach sub-Saharan Africa as effectively |

## Modern issues in agriculture

| Issue | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Agribusiness** | Large corporate-controlled food systems (Cargill, Tyson, ADM) |
| **Vertical integration** | One company controls multiple stages (seed → farm → processing → distribution) |
| **Monoculture** | Growing one crop on the same land each season — high yield, low resilience |
| **GMOs** | Genetically modified organisms — increased yield but controversial |
| **Organic farming** | No synthetic pesticides/fertilizers; smaller scale, premium price |
| **Sustainable agriculture** | Practices that maintain soil and ecosystem health long-term |
| **Food deserts** | Areas (often urban or low-income) without nearby access to fresh food |
| **Food insecurity** | Lack of consistent access to enough nutritious food |
| **Local food movements** | Farm-to-table, farmers' markets, CSAs — counter to globalized food |
| **Fair trade** | Certifies that producers get fair wages and working conditions |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing intensive and extensive farming** — intensive = lots of labor or capital on small land; extensive = small inputs on large land
- **Mixing up subsistence types** — shifting cultivation (tropics), intensive subsistence (Asia), pastoral nomadism (deserts) are very different
- **Forgetting von Thünen's assumptions** — flat land, one market, no technology — the model is theoretical
- **Calling all big farms "agribusiness"** — agribusiness specifically means corporate-controlled vertical integration
- **Treating the Green Revolution as all positive** — it boosted yields but caused environmental and social problems
- **Mixing up plantation and commercial gardening** — plantation = one cash crop in tropics; gardening = many fruits/veggies in temperate areas

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 5?**
Unit 5 makes up about 12–17% of multiple-choice questions and is a frequent FRQ unit. The von Thünen model, the Green Revolution, and the differences between subsistence and commercial agriculture are the most-tested topics.

**Is the von Thünen model still relevant today?**
The exact ring pattern doesn't appear in most real landscapes because of refrigeration, highways, and global shipping. But the underlying logic — perishable goods near cities, less perishable goods farther away — still holds. Dairy farms cluster near urban areas; ranching dominates remote regions.

**What's the difference between intensive and extensive farming?**
Intensive farming uses lots of labor or capital on a small amount of land (rice paddies in China, vegetable farms in California). Extensive farming uses small inputs over very large areas (cattle ranching in Wyoming, wheat farming in Kansas).

**Was the Green Revolution good or bad?**
Both. It dramatically increased food production and reduced hunger in countries like India and Mexico. But it also caused environmental damage (chemical runoff, soil depletion, water overuse), favored wealthier farmers, reduced crop diversity, and largely bypassed sub-Saharan Africa.

**Why is the U.S. Midwest a "mixed crop and livestock" region?**
The Midwest combines corn and soybean farming with hog and cattle raising. Crops feed the animals, and animal manure fertilizes the crops. The combination spreads economic risk and uses the land efficiently.

**What is shifting cultivation and where does it happen?**
Shifting cultivation (also called slash-and-burn) is when farmers clear a small patch of forest, burn the vegetation for nutrients, farm for a few years until soil is exhausted, then move on and let the forest regrow. It's common in tropical regions like the Amazon, the Congo Basin, and parts of Southeast Asia.
