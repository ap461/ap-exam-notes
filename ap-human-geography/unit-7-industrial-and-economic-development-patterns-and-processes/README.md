## What is Unit 7 about?

Unit 7 of AP Human Geography studies how economies develop over time and why some regions are wealthier than others. You'll learn how the Industrial Revolution transformed the world, the major theories of development (Rostow, Wallerstein, dependency theory), where industry locates and why (Weber's model), and how globalization reshapes labor, trade, and inequality. This unit makes up about 12–17% of the exam and is one of the most theory-heavy units. Expect FRQs that ask you to apply development models to specific countries or compare two theories.

## Key concepts to master

### The Industrial Revolution

- **Started in late 1700s in Britain** — coal, iron, and the steam engine
- **Spread to Europe and North America** in the 1800s
- **Spread to East Asia and beyond** in the 1900s and 2000s
- **Caused** rapid urbanization, factory work, mass migration, environmental change

### Sectors of the economy

| Sector | What it covers | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Extracting raw materials | Farming, mining, fishing, forestry |
| **Secondary** | Manufacturing and processing | Factories, construction, food processing |
| **Tertiary** | Services | Retail, healthcare, education, transport |
| **Quaternary** | Information and knowledge work | Finance, R&D, consulting, software |
| **Quinary** | Top-level decision-making | CEOs, government leaders, top researchers |

**Pattern:** as countries develop, jobs shift from primary → secondary → tertiary → quaternary/quinary.

## Major development theories

### Rostow's Stages of Growth (modernization theory)

A 5-stage model of how every country can develop, proposed by W.W. Rostow in 1960.

| Stage | Name | What's happening |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Traditional society | Subsistence agriculture, limited technology |
| **2** | Preconditions for takeoff | Infrastructure, education, leadership emerge |
| **3** | Takeoff | Industrialization begins, urbanization accelerates |
| **4** | Drive to maturity | Diversified economy, technology spreads |
| **5** | Age of high mass consumption | Service economy, consumer goods, high standard of living |

**Critique:** assumes every country follows the same path; ignores colonial history; modeled on the U.S./Europe experience.

### Wallerstein's World Systems Theory

The world is divided into three economic zones based on global trade relationships.

| Zone | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Core** | Wealthy, industrialized, dominant in trade and finance | U.S., Western Europe, Japan, South Korea |
| **Semi-periphery** | Industrializing, mid-income, gaining influence | Brazil, India, China, Mexico, Indonesia |
| **Periphery** | Poor, dependent on raw material exports | Most of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Central America |

**Key idea:** the global economy isn't a ladder every country climbs. It's a system where the core depends on the periphery for cheap labor and raw materials.

### Dependency Theory

A counter-theory to Rostow. Argues that periphery countries are poor not because they're "behind" but because the global system keeps them poor. Colonialism, debt, unfair trade, and corporate exploitation prevent development.

### Comparison of theories

| Theory | View | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rostow** | Optimistic — all countries can develop in stages | Simple, widely applied | Ignores colonialism, treats all countries the same |
| **Wallerstein** | Structural — global system creates inequality | Explains persistent inequality | Hard to test; less specific on how to escape periphery |
| **Dependency** | Critical — rich countries actively keep poor countries poor | Highlights power dynamics | Less clear about solutions |

## Industrial location: Weber's Least Cost Theory

Where should a factory be built? Weber said: where total costs are lowest. Three factors:

| Factor | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Transportation** | Cost to move raw materials in and finished products out | Heavy raw materials → factory near source (steel near iron) |
| **Labor** | Cost and availability of workers | Apparel factories in low-wage countries |
| **Agglomeration** | Benefits of clustering with related businesses | Auto industry in Detroit, tech in Silicon Valley |

### Weight-losing vs weight-gaining industries

- **Weight-losing (bulk-reducing)** — finished product is lighter than inputs. Locate near raw materials. (Steel, paper, copper smelting)
- **Weight-gaining (bulk-gaining)** — finished product is heavier than inputs. Locate near markets. (Soft drinks, cars assembled near consumers)

## Measuring development

| Measure | What it captures | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** | Total economic output inside a country | Doesn't measure quality of life, distribution |
| **GNI (Gross National Income)** | GDP + income from citizens abroad − foreign earnings inside country | More accurate for globalized economies |
| **GNI per capita** | Average income per person | Hides inequality |
| **HDI (Human Development Index)** | Combines income, education, and life expectancy | Better than GDP alone, but still misses inequality |
| **GII (Gender Inequality Index)** | Measures gender gaps in health, education, work | Still incomplete |
| **Gini coefficient** | Measures income inequality (0 = equal, 1 = total inequality) | Doesn't capture wealth (only income) |
| **Literacy rate** | % of adults who can read | Surface-level education metric |
| **Infant mortality rate** | Deaths under age 1 per 1,000 births | Strong indicator of healthcare quality |

## Globalization and trade

### Key concepts

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Globalization** | Increasing integration of economies, cultures, and politics |
| **Outsourcing** | Hiring outside companies to do work (often abroad) |
| **Offshoring** | Moving company operations to another country |
| **NICs (Newly Industrialized Countries)** | Mid-income countries rapidly industrializing — South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, China |
| **MNCs / TNCs** | Multinational / transnational corporations operating in multiple countries |
| **Free trade zones** | Areas with reduced tariffs to attract investment |
| **EPZs (Export Processing Zones)** | Zones where companies import materials, manufacture, and re-export with tax breaks |
| **Maquiladoras** | Mexican factories near the U.S. border doing assembly for U.S. markets |
| **Special Economic Zones (SEZs)** | China's version — designated cities with relaxed regulations |
| **NAFTA / USMCA** | Free trade agreement: U.S., Mexico, Canada |
| **WTO** | World Trade Organization — sets global trade rules |
| **Fordism** | Mass production using assembly lines and standardized products (early 20th century) |
| **Post-Fordism** | Flexible production, customized goods, global supply chains |
| **Just-in-time production** | Inventory delivered exactly when needed; minimizes warehouse costs (used by Toyota) |

## Sustainable development

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Sustainable development** | Meeting today's needs without compromising future generations |
| **UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** | 17 goals adopted in 2015 — poverty, hunger, education, climate, etc. |

## Women and development

- **Microfinance and microloans** — disproportionately help women entrepreneurs in developing countries
- **Female literacy** — strongly correlates with lower fertility, higher child health, and economic growth
- **Gender gap** — women earn less, own less property, and have less political representation worldwide
- **GII (Gender Inequality Index)** — captures gender disparities in development

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Treating Rostow as fact** — it's one theory, and many critics challenge it
- **Mixing up GDP and GNI** — GDP is what's produced inside a country; GNI accounts for cross-border income
- **Calling all rich countries "core"** — Wallerstein's framework is about trade relationships, not just wealth
- **Forgetting Weber's logic** — weight-losing industries near raw materials; weight-gaining near markets
- **Confusing outsourcing and offshoring** — outsourcing = hiring another company; offshoring = moving operations abroad (can be your own company)
- **Treating globalization as one-directional** — it lifts some economies and damages others; it's not all good or all bad

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 7?**
Unit 7 makes up about 12–17% of multiple-choice questions and is a heavy FRQ topic. Rostow's stages, Wallerstein's world systems, and Weber's least cost theory are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between Rostow and Wallerstein?**
Rostow is optimistic — every country can develop through 5 stages if it follows the right path. Wallerstein is structural — the global economy is divided into core, semi-periphery, and periphery, and the system keeps poor countries dependent on rich ones. Rostow says development is about effort; Wallerstein says it's about position in the global system.

**Why is HDI better than GDP?**
GDP only measures economic output. HDI combines income, life expectancy, and education — three dimensions of well-being. A country can have high GDP but poor health and education (oil-rich states), or modest GDP but strong human development (Cuba, Costa Rica). HDI captures the difference.

**What's the difference between core and periphery?**
Core countries are wealthy, industrialized, and dominate global trade and finance (U.S., Western Europe, Japan). Periphery countries are poor, dependent on raw material exports, and have limited control over their own economic future (much of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Latin America). Semi-periphery countries are in between (Brazil, India, China).

**Where should a steel mill be located, according to Weber?**
Steel is a weight-losing industry — iron ore and coal are heavy, but the finished steel weighs less. Weber's theory says locate near the raw materials to minimize transport costs. This is why historic steel cities like Pittsburgh and Birmingham (England) developed near coal and iron deposits.

**Is globalization good or bad for developing countries?**
Both. Globalization brings jobs, investment, and access to global markets. It can lift millions out of poverty (China's manufacturing rise). But it also creates dependency on foreign companies, exposes workers to exploitation, drives environmental damage, and makes economies vulnerable to global shocks. The AP exam expects you to recognize trade-offs.
