## What is Unit 6 about?

Unit 6 of AP Human Geography studies how cities form, grow, and organize themselves. You'll learn the patterns that explain why cities exist where they do (central place theory), how cities are structured internally (urban models), and the modern challenges cities face — gentrification, urban sprawl, housing affordability, environmental impact, and inequality. This unit makes up about 12–17% of the exam and is one of the most case-study-heavy units. Expect to apply concepts to specific cities like Detroit, Mexico City, Mumbai, and Atlanta.

## Key concepts to master

### Urbanization and city basics

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Urbanization** | Increase in the percentage of people living in cities |
| **Suburbanization** | Movement from city centers to surrounding suburbs |
| **Counter-urbanization** | Movement from cities back to rural areas |
| **Megacity** | A city with over 10 million residents |
| **Metacity** | A city with over 20 million residents |
| **Megalopolis** | A chain of connected metro areas (Boston–DC corridor) |
| **Conurbation** | Multiple cities that have grown together |
| **Site** | The physical features of a location (river, hill, harbor) |
| **Situation** | A location's relationship to other places (trade routes, neighbors) |

### Central Place Theory (Christaller)

Cities exist in a hierarchy based on the goods and services they provide. Larger cities are spaced farther apart and offer more specialized services.

| Concept | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Threshold** | Minimum population needed to support a service |
| **Range** | Maximum distance people will travel for a service |
| **Hexagonal hinterlands** | Theoretical market areas around each city |
| **High-order goods** | Specialized, expensive, infrequent (luxury cars, surgery) |
| **Low-order goods** | Common, cheap, frequent (groceries, gas) |

**Pattern:** small towns provide low-order goods. Bigger cities provide both low-order and high-order goods. The largest cities are spaced farthest apart.

### The rank-size rule and primate cities

- **Rank-size rule** — the nth largest city is 1/n the size of the largest. The 2nd city is half the largest, 3rd is one-third, etc. Common in countries with balanced urban systems (U.S., Germany).
- **Primate city** — one city is disproportionately larger than all others. Common in centralized states (Paris, London, Mexico City, Bangkok).

### The three classic urban models (North America)

| Model | Theorist | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Concentric Zone Model** | Burgess (1925, Chicago) | Rings: CBD → transition zone → working class → middle class → commuter zone |
| **Sector Model** | Hoyt (1939) | Wedges/sectors radiating out from CBD along transport corridors |
| **Multiple Nuclei Model** | Harris and Ullman (1945) | Multiple centers (CBD, industrial, university, suburban mall) |

**Galactic / Peripheral Model (Harris, 1960s):** edge cities and suburban nodes connected by highways — describes modern car-dependent metros (Atlanta, Phoenix).

### Urban models in the developing world

| Model | Region | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Latin American (Griffin-Ford)** | Latin America | Wealthy spine of development; squatter settlements on outskirts |
| **Sub-Saharan African (de Blij)** | Sub-Saharan Africa | Three CBDs (colonial, traditional, market); informal housing rings |
| **Southeast Asian (McGee)** | SE Asia | No formal CBD; centered on a port; mixed land uses |

### CBD (Central Business District)

- Highest land values, tallest buildings
- Concentrated retail, offices, services
- Often loses residential population (people commute in for work)
- Often the oldest part of the city
- Subject to peak land-value intersection (highest property values in the metro)

### Bid-rent theory

Land closer to the city center costs more. Different users (commercial, residential, agricultural) compete for land. The user willing to pay the most for proximity wins. Commercial wins downtown; residential takes the next ring; agriculture occupies the outermost (farthest from CBD).

## Urban challenges (modern issues)

| Issue | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Gentrification** | Higher-income residents move in, displacing lower-income residents and changing the neighborhood's character |
| **Urban sprawl** | Low-density, car-dependent expansion of cities outward |
| **Edge city** | Suburban node with offices, shopping, jobs (Tysons Corner VA, Schaumburg IL) |
| **Suburb** | Residential area outside city center, dependent on the city economically |
| **Exurb** | Even further out than suburbs; semi-rural, wealthy commuter areas |
| **White flight** | Movement of white residents from cities to suburbs (mid-20th century U.S.) |
| **Redlining** | Banks refusing loans in minority neighborhoods (illegal but historically influential) |
| **Blockbusting** | Real estate agents stoking fear to drive sales in changing neighborhoods |
| **Squatter settlements** | Informal housing on land residents don't own (favelas in Brazil, slums in Mumbai) |
| **Smart growth / new urbanism** | Compact, walkable, mixed-use development to counter sprawl |
| **Greenbelt** | Protected ring of undeveloped land around a city to limit sprawl (London, Seoul) |
| **Urban renewal** | Government-led redevelopment of older urban areas |

### Housing and inequality

- **Filtering** — homes pass from higher-income to lower-income owners over time as they age
- **Tipping point** — share of new residents that triggers rapid neighborhood change
- **Affordable housing** — homes that cost ≤ 30% of household income
- **Housing discrimination** — historical (redlining, restrictive covenants) and ongoing barriers

### Transportation and infrastructure

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Public transit** | Buses, subways, light rail — reduces sprawl and emissions |
| **Suburbanization of jobs** | Employment shifts from CBD to edge cities |
| **Commuter zones** | Outer rings where residents work in the city |
| **Brownfield** | Abandoned industrial land, often contaminated, redeveloped after cleanup |
| **Greenfield** | Undeveloped land on city outskirts converted to housing or commercial use |

### Environmental impact of cities

- **Urban heat island** — cities are warmer than surrounding areas due to concrete, dark roofs, low vegetation
- **Stormwater runoff** — paved surfaces create flooding
- **Air pollution** — concentrated emissions from traffic and industry
- **Sustainable cities** — incorporate green roofs, transit-oriented development, walkability, mixed-use zoning

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Mixing up urban models** — Concentric (rings, Burgess), Sector (wedges, Hoyt), Multiple Nuclei (multiple centers, Harris/Ullman)
- **Confusing megacity and primate city** — megacity is a size threshold (10M+); primate city is a relative dominance over other cities
- **Calling every big city a primate city** — the U.S. has no primate city (NYC and LA are similar in size)
- **Forgetting that gentrification has trade-offs** — improves infrastructure but displaces residents
- **Mixing up sprawl and suburbanization** — suburbanization is the movement of people; sprawl is the low-density spatial pattern that results
- **Treating informal settlements as failures** — favelas and slums house huge populations and have rich social networks; many are gradually formalized

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 6?**
Unit 6 makes up about 12–17% of multiple-choice questions and is a frequent FRQ topic. Urban models, gentrification, and central place theory are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between the three classic urban models?**
The Concentric Zone Model (Burgess) shows rings expanding out from the CBD. The Sector Model (Hoyt) shows wedges following transport routes. The Multiple Nuclei Model (Harris/Ullman) shows multiple centers, each specialized (industrial zone, university, suburb). All three were developed in 20th-century North America and best describe older U.S. cities.

**Is gentrification good or bad?**
Both. Gentrification brings investment, lower crime, and improved infrastructure to neglected neighborhoods. It also displaces lower-income residents who can no longer afford rent, erodes long-standing community ties, and changes neighborhood culture. The AP exam expects you to discuss both sides.

**What is a primate city?**
A primate city is one that is disproportionately larger than the second-largest city in the country, often more than twice as large. Primate cities tend to dominate economic, political, and cultural life. Examples include Paris (France), London (UK), Mexico City (Mexico), and Bangkok (Thailand). The U.S. does not have a primate city.

**What is the difference between a suburb and an exurb?**
Suburbs are residential areas just outside city centers, dependent on the city for jobs and services. Exurbs are even farther out — semi-rural, often wealthy, with long commutes to the city. Exurbs grew rapidly in the U.S. during the 1990s and 2000s with highway expansion.

**What causes urban sprawl?**
Urban sprawl results from cheap land at the city's edge, widespread car ownership, highway construction, low-density zoning, and tax policies that encourage single-family homes. Sprawl creates car dependence, longer commutes, environmental damage, and reduced public transit viability.
