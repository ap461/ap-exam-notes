# AP Biology Unit 6: Gene Expression and Regulation

> How DNA codes for proteins and how cells turn genes on and off. This unit covers DNA replication, transcription, translation, mutations, gene regulation (operons, transcription factors), and biotechnology tools like PCR, gel electrophoresis, and CRISPR.

## What is Unit 6 about?

Unit 6 of AP Biology studies how genetic information is stored in DNA, copied (replication), used to make proteins (transcription and translation), and regulated (turned on/off). You'll also learn about mutations, viruses, and the biotechnology tools scientists use to manipulate DNA — PCR, gel electrophoresis, restriction enzymes, and CRISPR. This unit makes up about 12–16% of the exam — one of the highest-weight units — and is the most central to modern biology. Expect FRQs on the central dogma, gene regulation, and lab techniques.

## Key concepts to master

### The central dogma

**DNA → RNA → Protein**

| Step | Process | Where it happens |
|---|---|---|
| **DNA → DNA** | Replication | Nucleus (eukaryotes) / cytoplasm (prokaryotes) |
| **DNA → RNA** | Transcription | Nucleus (eukaryotes) / cytoplasm (prokaryotes) |
| **RNA → Protein** | Translation | Ribosomes (cytoplasm or rough ER) |

## DNA structure (quick recap)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Building block** | Nucleotide (sugar + phosphate + base) |
| **Sugar** | Deoxyribose |
| **Bases** | A, T, G, C |
| **Base pairing** | A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds) |
| **Strands** | Double-stranded, antiparallel |
| **Direction** | Each strand runs 5' → 3' |
| **Backbone** | Sugar-phosphate, on the outside |

## DNA replication

DNA must be copied before a cell divides. Replication is **semiconservative** — each new DNA molecule has one original (parent) strand and one new strand.

### Key enzymes and proteins

| Enzyme/Protein | Function |
|---|---|
| **Helicase** | Unwinds the DNA double helix at the replication fork |
| **Topoisomerase** | Relieves tension ahead of the replication fork |
| **Single-strand binding proteins** | Keep separated strands apart |
| **Primase** | Adds RNA primer to start synthesis |
| **DNA polymerase III** | Adds DNA nucleotides 5' to 3'; the main builder |
| **DNA polymerase I** | Replaces RNA primers with DNA |
| **DNA ligase** | Seals gaps between Okazaki fragments |

### Leading vs lagging strand

DNA polymerase can only build **5' to 3'**. Because the strands are antiparallel, this creates a problem.

| Strand | How it's built |
|---|---|
| **Leading strand** | Built continuously toward the replication fork |
| **Lagging strand** | Built in short **Okazaki fragments**, away from the fork; later sealed by ligase |

### Direction of synthesis

- New DNA is built **5' to 3'**
- The template is read **3' to 5'**
- DNA polymerase only adds to the **3' end**

## Transcription (DNA → RNA)

Transcription copies a gene from DNA into mRNA so it can leave the nucleus and be translated.

### Three stages

| Stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Initiation** | RNA polymerase binds to the **promoter** region of the gene |
| **Elongation** | RNA polymerase reads DNA 3'→5' and builds mRNA 5'→3' |
| **Termination** | RNA polymerase reaches a **terminator sequence** and releases the mRNA |

### Key facts

- RNA uses **uracil (U)** instead of thymine (T)
- RNA is **single-stranded**
- Only one DNA strand (the **template strand**) is transcribed
- The **coding strand** has the same sequence as mRNA (except T → U)

---

## RNA processing (eukaryotes only)

After transcription, eukaryotic mRNA must be **processed** before leaving the nucleus.

| Modification | Function |
|---|---|
| **5' cap** | Added to the 5' end; protects mRNA and helps ribosomes recognize it |
| **Poly-A tail** | A string of A's added to the 3' end; protects mRNA and increases its lifespan |
| **Splicing** | **Introns (non-coding)** are removed; **exons (coding)** are joined together |
| **Alternative splicing** | Different combinations of exons are joined → one gene can make many different proteins |

**Memory tip:** **Ex**ons are **ex**pressed; **In**trons are **in** the trash.

**Why prokaryotes don't process mRNA:** they don't have a nucleus, so transcription and translation happen at the same time in the cytoplasm.

## Translation (RNA → Protein)

Translation reads mRNA and builds a protein at the ribosome.

### The genetic code

- **Codon** = 3 nucleotides on mRNA = 1 amino acid
- The code is **redundant** (multiple codons can code for the same amino acid)
- The code is **universal** (almost all organisms use the same code)
- **Start codon: AUG** (codes for methionine)
- **Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA** (don't code for amino acids; signal end of translation)

### Three stages of translation

| Stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Initiation** | Ribosome assembles around mRNA at the start codon (AUG); first tRNA arrives carrying methionine |
| **Elongation** | tRNAs bring amino acids; peptide bonds form between them; ribosome moves along mRNA codon by codon |
| **Termination** | Ribosome reaches a stop codon; protein is released; ribosome disassembles |

### Key players

| Player | Role |
|---|---|
| **mRNA** | Carries the genetic code |
| **tRNA** | Carries amino acids; has an anticodon that pairs with mRNA codon |
| **Ribosome** | Site of translation; made of rRNA and proteins |
| **Anticodon** | 3 nucleotides on tRNA; complementary to the mRNA codon |

### Ribosome sites

| Site | What happens |
|---|---|
| **A site (aminoacyl)** | Where new tRNA arrives carrying the next amino acid |
| **P site (peptidyl)** | Where the growing polypeptide chain sits |
| **E site (exit)** | Where empty tRNA leaves |

## Mutations

Changes in DNA sequence. Mutations are the **source of new alleles** — and the raw material for evolution.

### Types of point mutations

| Type | What changes |
|---|---|
| **Silent** | Base changes but amino acid stays the same (due to redundancy of code) |
| **Missense** | Base change → different amino acid |
| **Nonsense** | Base change → premature stop codon → shortened protein |

### Frameshift mutations

When bases are **inserted or deleted**, the reading frame shifts. Every codon after the mutation is wrong.

| Type | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Insertion** | Extra base added; usually severe |
| **Deletion** | Base removed; usually severe |

### Effects of mutations

- **Beneficial** — improves fitness (rare)
- **Neutral** — no effect on fitness
- **Harmful** — reduces fitness or causes disease

**Cancer connection:** mutations in proto-oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes can cause cancer (Unit 4).

## Gene regulation

Cells don't express all their genes all the time. Regulation determines which genes are turned on or off and at what level.

### Prokaryotic regulation: operons

An **operon** is a cluster of genes regulated together. Best-known examples:

| Operon | Type | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **lac operon** | Inducible | Normally **OFF**; turned **ON** when lactose is present (allows bacteria to digest lactose) |
| **trp operon** | Repressible | Normally **ON**; turned **OFF** when tryptophan is high (no need to make more) |

**Operon parts:**

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| **Promoter** | Where RNA polymerase binds |
| **Operator** | Where the repressor binds (acts as on/off switch) |
| **Structural genes** | The genes that actually code for proteins |
| **Repressor protein** | Binds to operator to block transcription |
| **Inducer / corepressor** | Small molecules that turn the operon on or off |

### Eukaryotic regulation

More complex than in prokaryotes — happens at multiple levels:

| Level | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Chromatin remodeling** | DNA wrapped tightly (heterochromatin) = off; loosely (euchromatin) = on |
| **DNA methylation** | Methyl groups added → genes silenced |
| **Histone modification** | Acetylation loosens DNA → genes more active |
| **Transcription factors** | Proteins that bind to DNA to activate or repress transcription |
| **Enhancers and silencers** | DNA sequences that increase or decrease transcription |
| **Alternative splicing** | Different exon combinations → different proteins from same gene |
| **mRNA stability** | How long mRNA lasts in cytoplasm affects protein production |
| **Translational regulation** | Whether and how often mRNA is translated |
| **Post-translational modification** | Folding, phosphorylation, cleavage of the finished protein |

---

## Epigenetics

Changes in gene expression **without changing the DNA sequence**. Often heritable, often reversible.

| Mechanism | Effect |
|---|---|
| **DNA methylation** | Adds methyl groups to cytosine → gene silenced |
| **Histone acetylation** | Loosens chromatin → gene active |
| **Histone methylation** | Can either silence or activate, depending on the histone |

**Why it matters:** environmental factors (stress, diet, toxins) can alter epigenetic marks, sometimes affecting offspring.

## Viruses

Viruses are **not cells** — they're genetic material (DNA or RNA) inside a protein coat (capsid). They can only reproduce inside a host cell.

### Two replication cycles (in bacteriophages)

| Cycle | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Lytic cycle** | Virus replicates rapidly, lyses (bursts) the host cell, releases new viruses |
| **Lysogenic cycle** | Viral DNA integrates into host genome (as **prophage**); copied with host's DNA; can later switch to lytic cycle |

### Types of viruses

| Type | What it does |
|---|---|
| **DNA virus** | Uses host's machinery directly to replicate |
| **RNA virus** | Must replicate RNA → RNA (using own polymerase) |
| **Retrovirus** | RNA → DNA using **reverse transcriptase**; integrates DNA into host genome (e.g., HIV) |

## Biotechnology

Tools and techniques scientists use to manipulate DNA.

### Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)

Amplifies a specific DNA sequence — making millions of copies from a tiny sample.

**Steps (one cycle):**

1. **Denaturation (95°C)** — DNA strands separate
2. **Annealing (55°C)** — primers bind to target sequences
3. **Extension (72°C)** — Taq polymerase builds new strands

**Repeat 30+ cycles → exponential amplification.**

### Gel electrophoresis

Separates DNA fragments by **size**.

- Smaller fragments move **farther** through the gel
- Larger fragments move **less**
- DNA is negatively charged → moves toward the **positive** electrode
- Used to compare DNA samples (paternity tests, forensics, identifying species)

### Restriction enzymes

Cut DNA at specific sequences (called **recognition sites**). Used to:

- Make recombinant DNA (combining DNA from two sources)
- Create DNA fragments for cloning or sequencing

### Genetic engineering and CRISPR

| Technique | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Recombinant DNA** | Combine DNA from different sources (e.g., human insulin gene in bacteria) |
| **Plasmids** | Circular DNA used as vectors to insert genes into bacteria |
| **CRISPR-Cas9** | Edits genes precisely; cuts DNA at a target sequence using a guide RNA |
| **Gene therapy** | Replacing or correcting faulty genes in patients |
| **Cloning** | Producing genetically identical organisms |

### DNA sequencing

Determines the order of nucleotides in a DNA sample. Modern sequencing is fast and cheap, enabling personalized medicine and large-scale genomics.

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing the template and coding strands** — template is read by RNA polymerase (3'→5'); coding strand has the same sequence as mRNA (except T→U)
- **Forgetting that DNA is built 5'→3'** — DNA polymerase only adds to the 3' end
- **Mixing up introns and exons** — **ex**ons are **ex**pressed; **in**trons are removed
- **Forgetting RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T)**
- **Confusing the lac and trp operons** — lac is inducible (off until lactose); trp is repressible (on until tryptophan)
- **Calling a virus a cell** — viruses are not cells; they can't reproduce on their own
- **Forgetting the difference between mutation types** — silent (no change), missense (different amino acid), nonsense (premature stop), frameshift (everything after is wrong)
- **Mixing up transcription and translation** — transcription = DNA → RNA; translation = RNA → protein

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 6?**
Unit 6 makes up about 12–16% of multiple-choice questions and is one of the most-tested units overall. The central dogma, gene regulation, and biotechnology techniques are the most-tested concepts.

**What is the central dogma of biology?**
The central dogma describes the flow of genetic information: DNA → RNA → Protein. DNA is copied (replication), used to make RNA (transcription), and RNA is used to make proteins (translation). Retroviruses are an exception — they use reverse transcriptase to go RNA → DNA.

**Why is DNA replication called semiconservative?**
After replication, each new DNA double helix contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand. The original strand is "conserved" while a new strand is built next to it. This was confirmed by the famous Meselson-Stahl experiment in 1958.

**What's the difference between an inducible and repressible operon?**
An inducible operon (like lac) is normally **off** and is turned on when an inducer (like lactose) is present. A repressible operon (like trp) is normally **on** and is turned off when a corepressor (like tryptophan) is present. Inducible operons handle digestion of substrates available sometimes; repressible operons handle synthesis of products needed sometimes.

**What's the difference between introns and exons?**
Exons are the parts of a gene that **code for protein** — they are kept after RNA processing. Introns are non-coding regions that are **removed** during splicing. The exons are joined together to form the mature mRNA. Alternative splicing — combining different exons in different ways — allows one gene to make multiple proteins.

**How does a missense mutation differ from a nonsense mutation?**
A missense mutation changes one amino acid in the protein (a different amino acid is now coded for). A nonsense mutation creates a premature stop codon, which usually produces a truncated, nonfunctional protein. Nonsense mutations are typically more severe than missense mutations.

**What is CRISPR and why is it important?**
CRISPR-Cas9 is a gene-editing tool that lets scientists cut DNA at a specific location and either disable a gene or replace it with a new sequence. It's based on a natural defense system bacteria use against viruses. CRISPR is faster, cheaper, and more precise than older gene-editing methods, and it's transforming research, medicine (treating genetic diseases), and agriculture.

**Why are viruses considered "not alive"?**
Viruses lack many features of life: they can't reproduce on their own (they need a host cell's machinery), they don't have cells, they don't carry out metabolism, they don't grow, and they don't respond to stimuli the way living things do. They're closer to a complex molecular package than a living organism — though they evolve and have a major impact on living systems.
