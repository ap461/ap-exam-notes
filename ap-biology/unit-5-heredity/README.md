# AP Biology Unit 5: Heredity

> How traits are passed from parents to offspring and how genetic variation arises. This unit covers meiosis, Mendelian genetics, Punnett squares, sex-linked inheritance, non-Mendelian patterns, and chromosomal disorders.

## What is Unit 5 about?

Unit 5 of AP Biology studies how genetic information is passed from parents to offspring. You'll learn how meiosis produces gametes with genetic variation, how Mendel's laws explain inheritance patterns, how to use Punnett squares and pedigrees to predict offspring, and how non-Mendelian patterns (incomplete dominance, codominance, polygenic traits, sex-linked traits) work. This unit makes up about 8–11% of the exam and is one of the most math-heavy units — expect to calculate probabilities and use the chi-square test on FRQs.

## Key concepts to master

### Genetics vocabulary

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Gene** | A segment of DNA that codes for a trait |
| **Allele** | A version of a gene (e.g., dominant or recessive) |
| **Genotype** | The combination of alleles a person has (e.g., Aa, BB) |
| **Phenotype** | The physical expression of the genotype (e.g., brown eyes, tall) |
| **Homozygous** | Two identical alleles (AA or aa) |
| **Heterozygous** | Two different alleles (Aa) |
| **Dominant** | An allele that masks the recessive when both are present (uppercase letter) |
| **Recessive** | An allele only expressed when homozygous (lowercase letter) |
| **Locus** | The location of a gene on a chromosome |
| **Diploid (2n)** | Two sets of chromosomes (e.g., human body cells, 46 chromosomes) |
| **Haploid (n)** | One set of chromosomes (e.g., gametes, 23 chromosomes) |

## Meiosis (the source of genetic variation)

Meiosis produces **4 genetically different haploid gametes** from one diploid cell. It involves **two divisions** (Meiosis I and Meiosis II).

### Meiosis I (the reductional division)

Homologous chromosomes separate. This is where the chromosome number is halved.

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Prophase I** | Chromosomes condense; **homologous pairs** form (synapsis); **crossing over** occurs at chiasmata |
| **Metaphase I** | Homologous pairs line up at the equator (independent assortment) |
| **Anaphase I** | Homologous chromosomes separate; sister chromatids stay together |
| **Telophase I + Cytokinesis** | Two haploid cells form (each with sister chromatids) |

### Meiosis II (like mitosis, but with haploid cells)

Sister chromatids separate.

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Prophase II** | Chromosomes recondense |
| **Metaphase II** | Chromosomes line up at equator |
| **Anaphase II** | Sister chromatids separate |
| **Telophase II + Cytokinesis** | 4 haploid daughter cells |

### Sources of genetic variation in meiosis

| Source | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Crossing over** | Homologous chromosomes exchange segments in Prophase I |
| **Independent assortment** | Maternal and paternal chromosomes line up randomly in Metaphase I |
| **Random fertilization** | Any sperm can fertilize any egg |

These three sources combined create essentially infinite genetic variation in offspring.

## Mitosis vs Meiosis

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Growth, repair, asexual reproduction | Sexual reproduction (gamete production) |
| **Number of divisions** | 1 | 2 |
| **Daughter cells** | 2 | 4 |
| **Chromosome number** | Same as parent (diploid) | Half of parent (haploid) |
| **Genetic variation** | Identical to parent | Genetically unique |
| **Crossing over** | No | Yes |
| **Where it happens** | Body (somatic) cells | Reproductive cells (testes, ovaries) |

## Mendel's laws

### Law of Segregation

The two alleles for a trait separate during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one allele.

### Law of Independent Assortment

Alleles for different traits sort independently of each other (true for genes on different chromosomes — linked genes are an exception).

### Law of Dominance

When two different alleles are present, the dominant one is expressed in the phenotype.

## Punnett squares

A grid that predicts the probability of offspring genotypes and phenotypes.

### Monohybrid cross (one trait)

**Aa × Aa** →

| | A | a |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | AA | Aa |
| **a** | Aa | aa |

**Genotype ratio:** 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa
**Phenotype ratio:** 3 dominant : 1 recessive

### Dihybrid cross (two traits)

**AaBb × AaBb** → **9:3:3:1** phenotype ratio

| Phenotype | Ratio |
|---|---|
| Both dominant | 9 |
| First dominant, second recessive | 3 |
| First recessive, second dominant | 3 |
| Both recessive | 1 |

### Test cross

Cross an unknown dominant phenotype with a homozygous recessive (aa) to figure out if the unknown is **AA** or **Aa**.

| Cross | If unknown is AA | If unknown is Aa |
|---|---|---|
| **? × aa** | All offspring dominant phenotype | 1:1 dominant : recessive |

## Sex-linked inheritance

Genes on **X or Y chromosomes**. The X chromosome carries many more genes than the Y, so most "sex-linked" traits are X-linked.

| Trait | Carried on | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **X-linked recessive** | X chromosome | Males (XY) only need 1 recessive allele to express; females (XX) need 2 |
| **X-linked dominant** | X chromosome | Affected fathers pass to all daughters, no sons |
| **Y-linked** | Y chromosome | Father → son only |

**Examples:**
- **Hemophilia** — X-linked recessive
- **Color blindness** — X-linked recessive
- **Duchenne muscular dystrophy** — X-linked recessive

**Key fact:** males are more likely to express X-linked recessive traits because they only have one X chromosome (XY) — no second X to mask the recessive allele.

## Non-Mendelian inheritance

Patterns that don't follow simple dominant/recessive inheritance.

### Incomplete dominance

Heterozygotes show a **blended** phenotype (neither allele is fully dominant).

**Example:** Red flower (RR) × White flower (rr) → Pink flowers (Rr)

### Codominance

Both alleles are **fully expressed** at the same time (not blended).

**Example:** Red bull (RR) × White cow (WW) → Roan offspring (RW) showing both red and white hairs.

### Multiple alleles

A gene has **more than two possible alleles** in the population.

**Example: ABO blood type**

| Genotype | Blood type |
|---|---|
| Iᴬ Iᴬ or Iᴬ i | Type A |
| Iᴮ Iᴮ or Iᴮ i | Type B |
| Iᴬ Iᴮ | Type AB (codominant) |
| ii | Type O |

### Polygenic traits

Multiple genes contribute to one trait. Produces a **continuous range** of phenotypes (a bell curve).

**Examples:** height, skin color, eye color, weight

### Pleiotropy

One gene affects **multiple traits**.

**Example:** Sickle-cell anemia — one gene mutation affects red blood cells, oxygen transport, immune function, and circulation.

### Epistasis

One gene **masks or modifies** the expression of another gene.

**Example:** Coat color in Labrador retrievers — a separate gene determines whether the coat is colored or yellow regardless of the brown/black gene.

### Linked genes

Genes on the **same chromosome** tend to be inherited together (violating independent assortment). They can be separated by **crossing over** during Prophase I.

## Probability rules

| Rule | Use when | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **Multiplication rule (AND)** | Two events both happen | P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) |
| **Addition rule (OR)** | Either event happens (mutually exclusive) | P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) |

**Example:** Probability of a child being female AND having brown eyes = P(female) × P(brown eyes) = ½ × ¾ = ⅜

## Chi-square (χ²) test

Determines if observed results match expected results from a genetic cross.

**Formula:** χ² = Σ [(observed − expected)² ÷ expected]

**Steps:**

1. Calculate expected ratios from the predicted cross
2. Compare observed to expected using the formula
3. Compare χ² value to critical value (df = number of categories − 1)
4. **If χ² < critical value:** results match the prediction (fail to reject null hypothesis)
5. **If χ² > critical value:** results don't match (reject null hypothesis)

**Standard significance level:** p = 0.05

The chi-square table is provided on the AP Biology formula sheet.

## Pedigree analysis

A pedigree chart shows inheritance patterns through generations.

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Square | Male |
| Circle | Female |
| Filled shape | Affected (shows the trait) |
| Empty shape | Unaffected |
| Half-filled | Carrier |
| Horizontal line | Mating |
| Vertical line | Offspring |

**Determining the inheritance pattern:**

| Pattern | Clues |
|---|---|
| **Autosomal recessive** | Skips generations; affected parents → unaffected children rare; both sexes affected equally |
| **Autosomal dominant** | Affected in every generation; affected parent → ~50% affected children; both sexes equally |
| **X-linked recessive** | More affected males; never father → son; carrier mothers → affected sons |
| **X-linked dominant** | Affected father → all daughters affected, no sons; both sexes affected |

## Chromosomal disorders

When errors in meiosis cause **abnormal chromosome numbers** (nondisjunction).

### Nondisjunction

Chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis. Results in gametes with extra or missing chromosomes.

| Disorder | Cause | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Down syndrome** | Trisomy 21 (extra chromosome 21) | Most common chromosomal disorder |
| **Turner syndrome** | XO (missing one X) | Females; short stature, infertility |
| **Klinefelter syndrome** | XXY | Males; reduced fertility |
| **Patau syndrome** | Trisomy 13 | Severe, often fatal early |
| **Edwards syndrome** | Trisomy 18 | Severe, often fatal early |

### Other chromosomal mutations

| Mutation | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Deletion** | A chunk of chromosome is lost |
| **Duplication** | A chunk is repeated |
| **Inversion** | A chunk is flipped |
| **Translocation** | A chunk moves to a different chromosome |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Forgetting that meiosis = 2 divisions** — this is what makes it different from mitosis
- **Confusing crossing over and independent assortment** — crossing over swaps DNA between homologs (Prophase I); independent assortment is random orientation of homolog pairs (Metaphase I)
- **Mixing up homozygous and heterozygous** — homo = same; hetero = different
- **Confusing genotype and phenotype** — genotype is the alleles (Aa); phenotype is the trait (brown eyes)
- **Forgetting the dihybrid 9:3:3:1 ratio** — this is the standard answer for two-trait crosses with heterozygotes
- **Mixing up incomplete dominance and codominance** — incomplete dominance blends (red + white = pink); codominance shows both (red + white = roan, with both red and white hairs)
- **Saying X-linked traits are inherited from fathers** — fathers pass X to daughters and Y to sons; sons inherit X-linked traits from their mothers
- **Forgetting that humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs)** — gametes have 23 (haploid)

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 5?**
Unit 5 makes up about 8–11% of multiple-choice questions and is one of the most common FRQ topics. Punnett squares, chi-square tests, pedigree analysis, and meiosis are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between incomplete dominance and codominance?**
Incomplete dominance produces a **blended** phenotype — neither allele is fully expressed (red + white flowers = pink). Codominance shows **both alleles fully** at the same time, side by side (red + white cow = roan with patches of both red and white hairs, no pink).

**Why are males more likely to have X-linked recessive disorders?**
Males have only one X chromosome (XY). If they inherit one recessive allele on that X, they express the trait — there's no second X to mask it. Females have two X chromosomes (XX), so they need two recessive alleles to express the trait. This is why hemophilia and color blindness are much more common in males.

**What is crossing over and why is it important?**
Crossing over happens in Prophase I of meiosis. Homologous chromosomes line up and exchange segments at points called chiasmata. This shuffles maternal and paternal alleles into new combinations, creating genetic variation in gametes. Without crossing over, sexual reproduction would produce far less diversity.

**How do I know which inheritance pattern a pedigree shows?**
Look at four things: (1) Does it skip generations? Skipping = recessive. (2) Are both sexes affected equally? Equal = autosomal. (3) Are more males affected? More males = X-linked recessive. (4) Does an affected father always pass to all daughters? Yes = X-linked dominant. Walking through these four questions usually identifies the pattern.

**What is nondisjunction?**
Nondisjunction is when chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis. The result is gametes with too many or too few chromosomes. When such a gamete fertilizes a normal one, the resulting zygote has an abnormal chromosome number — leading to disorders like Down syndrome (extra chromosome 21), Turner syndrome (missing X), or Klinefelter syndrome (extra X).

**Are skin color and height controlled by one gene or many?**
Many. Skin color and height are **polygenic traits** — multiple genes contribute, often with environmental factors. This is why height and skin color show a continuous range (a bell curve), not just a few categories. Mendel's pea plants had simple single-gene traits, but most human traits are polygenic.

**What is the chi-square test used for?**
The chi-square test compares observed genetic ratios to expected ratios from a Mendelian cross. If the observed and expected match closely, the chi-square value is small and we accept the prediction. If they don't match, the chi-square value is large and we reject the prediction — meaning the trait may not follow simple Mendelian inheritance, or there could be linkage, lethality, or another factor at play.
