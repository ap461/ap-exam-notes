# AP Biology Unit 8: Ecology

> How organisms interact with each other and their environment at every scale. This unit covers population ecology, community interactions, food webs, energy flow, biogeochemical cycles, ecosystem disturbances, biodiversity, and human impact on ecosystems.

## What is Unit 8 about?

Unit 8 of AP Biology studies the interactions between organisms and their environments at multiple levels — populations, communities, ecosystems, and the biosphere. You'll learn how populations grow, how species interact (competition, predation, symbiosis), how energy flows through food webs, how nutrients cycle through ecosystems, and how human activities like climate change, pollution, and habitat loss affect biodiversity. This unit makes up about 10–15% of the exam and often appears in FRQs that combine ecology with evolution, data analysis, and graph interpretation.

## Key concepts to master

### Levels of ecological organization

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Organism** | A single individual |
| **Population** | All individuals of one species in an area |
| **Community** | All populations of different species in an area |
| **Ecosystem** | Community + abiotic environment (water, soil, air) |
| **Biome** | Large region with similar climate and life forms |
| **Biosphere** | All ecosystems on Earth combined |

## Population ecology

### Growth models

| Model | Equation | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Exponential growth** | dN/dt = rN | J-shaped curve; unlimited resources; rare in nature |
| **Logistic growth** | dN/dt = rN[(K-N)/K] | S-shaped curve; resources limited; growth slows as N approaches K |

| Variable | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **N** | Population size |
| **r** | Per capita growth rate (births − deaths per individual) |
| **K** | Carrying capacity (max sustainable population) |
| **dN/dt** | Rate of population change over time |

### Carrying capacity (K)

The maximum population an environment can sustain. Determined by available resources (food, water, space, light). Populations fluctuate around K based on density-dependent and density-independent factors.

### Limiting factors

| Type | What it is | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Density-dependent** | Effect changes with population density | Competition, disease, predation, food availability |
| **Density-independent** | Effect is the same regardless of density | Natural disasters, weather, fires |

### Survivorship curves

Graphs of how survivorship changes with age.

| Type | Pattern | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Type I** | High early survival, sharp drop late in life | Humans, large mammals (lots of parental care) |
| **Type II** | Constant death rate at all ages | Birds, small mammals |
| **Type III** | High early death, then long life | Fish, insects, plants (many offspring, no care) |

### Reproductive strategies (r vs K)

| Strategy | Features | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **r-selected** | Many offspring, little parental care, short lives, fast maturity | Insects, fish, weeds |
| **K-selected** | Few offspring, lots of parental care, long lives, slow maturity | Humans, elephants, oak trees |

## Community ecology

### Species interactions

| Interaction | Effect on species 1 | Effect on species 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Competition** | − | − |
| **Predation** | + | − |
| **Parasitism** | + | − |
| **Mutualism** | + | + |
| **Commensalism** | + | 0 |

### Competition

| Type | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Interspecific** | Between different species |
| **Intraspecific** | Within the same species |
| **Competitive exclusion principle** | Two species with identical niches can't coexist long-term — one will outcompete the other |
| **Resource partitioning** | Species evolve to use different parts of a resource (different food sources, feeding times, microhabitats) |
| **Niche** | All abiotic + biotic conditions a species needs |
| **Fundamental niche** | Where a species **could** live with no competition |
| **Realized niche** | Where it **actually** lives, given competition and predators |

### Predation defenses

| Defense | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cryptic coloration (camouflage)** | Blending in with environment | Walking sticks, arctic hare |
| **Aposematic (warning) coloration** | Bright colors signal toxicity | Poison dart frog, monarch butterfly |
| **Batesian mimicry** | Harmless species mimics dangerous one | Viceroy butterfly mimics monarch |
| **Müllerian mimicry** | Two harmful species mimic each other | Multiple bee/wasp species look similar |
| **Mechanical defenses** | Spines, shells, thorns | Cactus, porcupine, turtle |
| **Chemical defenses** | Toxins, bad taste | Skunks, poison ivy |

### Symbiosis

A close, long-term relationship between two species.

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mutualism** | Both benefit | Bees and flowers; coral and zooxanthellae; gut bacteria and humans |
| **Commensalism** | One benefits, other unaffected | Barnacles on whales; orchids on trees |
| **Parasitism** | One benefits, other harmed | Tapeworms; ticks; mistletoe |

### Keystone species

A species whose **removal would dramatically change** the community structure. Often a top predator or critical resource provider.

**Examples:**
- **Sea otters** in kelp forests (eat sea urchins, prevent overgrazing of kelp)
- **Wolves** in Yellowstone (control elk populations, allow vegetation to recover)
- **Beavers** (engineer wetland habitats with dams)

## Ecosystem ecology

### Energy flow

Energy enters ecosystems as **sunlight** (or chemical energy in deep sea vents). It flows in **one direction**: producers → consumers → decomposers, with energy lost as heat at each step.

### Trophic levels

| Level | Role | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary producers (autotrophs)** | Make their own food (photosynthesis or chemosynthesis) | Plants, algae, cyanobacteria |
| **Primary consumers (herbivores)** | Eat producers | Cows, deer, zooplankton |
| **Secondary consumers** | Eat primary consumers | Frogs, small fish |
| **Tertiary consumers** | Eat secondary consumers | Snakes, large fish |
| **Apex predators** | Top of food chain | Wolves, eagles, sharks |
| **Decomposers** | Break down dead organisms | Bacteria, fungi |

### The 10% rule

Only about **10% of energy** transfers from one trophic level to the next. The other 90% is lost as **heat** (during cellular respiration), used for movement, or stored in indigestible parts.

**Implication:** food chains are usually limited to **4–5 trophic levels** because too little energy is left at the top.

### Ecological pyramids

| Pyramid | What it shows | Always upright? |
|---|---|---|
| **Pyramid of energy** | Energy at each level | Always upright (10% rule) |
| **Pyramid of biomass** | Mass of organisms at each level | Usually upright; can be inverted in aquatic systems |
| **Pyramid of numbers** | Number of organisms at each level | Can be inverted (one tree supports many insects) |

## Biogeochemical cycles

Nutrients cycle between living organisms and the environment.

### The water cycle

Driven by sun energy. Includes evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration. **The fastest-cycling nutrient.**

### The carbon cycle

| Process | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Photosynthesis** | Removes CO₂ from atmosphere; stores carbon in plants |
| **Cellular respiration** | Returns CO₂ to atmosphere |
| **Decomposition** | Returns carbon to soil and atmosphere |
| **Combustion** | Burning fossil fuels rapidly releases stored carbon as CO₂ |
| **Ocean exchange** | Oceans absorb and release CO₂; act as a carbon sink |

**Human impact:** burning fossil fuels and deforestation increase atmospheric CO₂ → climate change.

### The nitrogen cycle

Most complex cycle. Atmosphere is 78% N₂, but most organisms can't use it directly.

| Process | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Nitrogen fixation** | Bacteria (e.g., *Rhizobium* in legume roots) convert N₂ → ammonia (NH₃) |
| **Nitrification** | Bacteria convert ammonia → nitrites → nitrates (NO₃⁻) — usable form for plants |
| **Assimilation** | Plants take up nitrates and incorporate into proteins, DNA |
| **Ammonification** | Decomposers break down dead organisms → ammonia |
| **Denitrification** | Bacteria convert nitrates → N₂ → atmosphere |

**Human impact:** synthetic fertilizers add excess nitrogen → runoff → eutrophication and dead zones.

### The phosphorus cycle

No atmospheric component. Phosphorus moves between rocks, soil, water, and organisms.

| Process | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Weathering** | Rocks break down → release phosphate into soil and water |
| **Plant uptake** | Plants absorb phosphate; passes up the food chain |
| **Decomposition** | Phosphorus returns to soil |
| **Sedimentation** | Phosphorus accumulates in ocean sediments → eventually becomes rock again |

**Human impact:** fertilizer runoff → algal blooms → oxygen depletion in lakes and oceans.

## Ecosystem disturbances

### Ecological succession

The recovery and change in species composition over time after a disturbance.

| Type | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Primary succession** | Starts on bare rock with no soil (after volcano, glacier retreat); takes hundreds to thousands of years |
| **Secondary succession** | Starts on existing soil after disturbance (fire, flood, agriculture); faster than primary |

**Pioneer species** are first to colonize (lichens, mosses, grasses). They modify the environment, allowing later species (shrubs, trees) to establish — leading toward a **climax community** (mature, stable ecosystem).

### Major disturbances

| Disturbance | Cause |
|---|---|
| **Fire** | Natural or human-caused; some ecosystems require it (chaparral, grasslands) |
| **Flooding** | Storms, dam failure, climate change |
| **Drought** | Long-term climate; changes vegetation |
| **Storms** | Hurricanes, tornadoes; reset succession |
| **Volcanic eruption** | Wipes out life over wide areas |
| **Human activities** | Deforestation, pollution, urbanization, agriculture |

## Biodiversity

The variety of life at three levels:

| Level | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Genetic diversity** | Variation in genes within a species |
| **Species diversity** | Variety of species in an area |
| **Ecosystem diversity** | Variety of habitats and ecosystems |

### Why biodiversity matters

- **Ecosystem services** — clean water, pollination, climate regulation, soil formation
- **Resilience** — diverse ecosystems recover better from disturbance
- **Medicine and food** — wild species are sources of drugs, crops, genetic resources
- **Cultural and intrinsic value** — natural beauty, recreation, ethical importance

### Threats to biodiversity (HIPPO)

A common acronym for major threats:

- **H — Habitat destruction** (deforestation, urbanization)
- **I — Invasive species** (outcompete or prey on natives)
- **P — Pollution** (air, water, soil, noise, light)
- **P — Population growth** (human population pressure on resources)
- **O — Overexploitation** (overfishing, hunting, harvesting)

## Climate change and human impact

### Greenhouse effect

Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane CH₄, nitrous oxide N₂O, water vapor) trap heat in the atmosphere. The natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm enough for life. Human activities have **increased** greenhouse gas concentrations, intensifying warming.

### Major causes of climate change

| Cause | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Burning fossil fuels** | Releases CO₂ — primary driver of warming |
| **Deforestation** | Reduces CO₂ uptake; releases stored carbon when forests are cleared/burned |
| **Agriculture** | Methane from livestock, rice paddies; nitrous oxide from fertilizers |
| **Industry** | CO₂ and other emissions from manufacturing |

### Effects of climate change

- **Rising temperatures** globally
- **Sea level rise** — melting ice + thermal expansion
- **Ocean acidification** — CO₂ dissolves in water → carbonic acid → harms shell-forming organisms
- **Shifting species ranges** — species moving toward poles or to higher elevations
- **Phenological mismatches** — flowers blooming before pollinators arrive
- **Extreme weather** — more intense storms, droughts, heat waves
- **Coral bleaching** — warming ocean stresses coral, expelling zooxanthellae
- **Habitat loss** — Arctic ice loss, coastal erosion

### Other human impacts

| Impact | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Eutrophication** | Excess nutrients (from fertilizer/sewage) → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → dead zones |
| **Biomagnification** | Toxins (DDT, mercury) increase in concentration up the food chain |
| **Ozone depletion** | CFCs degraded ozone layer; allowed more UV through; mostly recovering since Montreal Protocol |
| **Plastic pollution** | Microplastics throughout food web; ocean garbage patches |
| **Invasive species** | Disrupt native ecosystems (zebra mussels, kudzu, cane toads) |
| **Light and noise pollution** | Disrupts animal behavior, migration, reproduction |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing food chain and food web** — chain shows one path; web shows all interconnected paths
- **Forgetting the 10% rule** — 90% of energy is lost between trophic levels
- **Mixing up r-selected and K-selected** — r = many offspring, little care; K = few offspring, lots of care
- **Calling all species interactions "symbiosis"** — symbiosis is specifically close, long-term interaction
- **Confusing fundamental and realized niche** — fundamental = could live; realized = actually lives (given competition)
- **Mixing up density-dependent and independent** — dependent changes with density (disease, food); independent doesn't (weather, fires)
- **Forgetting that energy flows but nutrients cycle** — energy enters as sunlight and exits as heat; nutrients (C, N, P) cycle through ecosystems repeatedly
- **Saying decomposers are at the top of food chain** — decomposers operate at every level, breaking down dead matter

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 8?**
Unit 8 makes up about 10–15% of multiple-choice questions and frequently appears in FRQs, especially data analysis questions. Population growth, food webs, biogeochemical cycles, and human impacts on ecosystems are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between exponential and logistic growth?**
Exponential growth (J-curve) happens when resources are unlimited and the population grows faster and faster. Logistic growth (S-curve) happens when resources become limited — growth starts fast but slows as the population approaches its carrying capacity (K). Real populations almost always follow logistic growth, with fluctuations around K.

**What is a keystone species?**
A keystone species has an outsized effect on its community relative to its abundance. Removing a keystone species causes major changes in the ecosystem. Sea otters keep sea urchin populations in check, allowing kelp forests to thrive — without otters, urchins overgraze and kelp forests collapse. Wolves in Yellowstone control elk, which in turn allows trees and beavers to recover.

**What is the 10% rule?**
Only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level transfers to the next. The other 90% is lost as heat through respiration, used for movement, or stored in parts that aren't eaten. This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels — there's not enough energy left at the top to support more.

**What's the difference between primary and secondary succession?**
Primary succession starts on bare rock or sterile substrate where there's no soil — like after a volcanic eruption or a glacier retreating. Pioneer species like lichens slowly build soil over hundreds or thousands of years. Secondary succession starts on existing soil after a disturbance like fire or flooding. It's much faster because soil and seeds are already in place — recovery can take decades, not millennia.

**Why is the nitrogen cycle so complicated?**
Atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) makes up 78% of the air, but most organisms can't use it directly because the triple bond between two N atoms is extremely hard to break. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria are the only organisms that can convert N₂ into usable forms (ammonia). Then nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia to nitrate, plants take up nitrate, animals eat plants, decomposers return nitrogen to the soil, and denitrifying bacteria eventually return it to the atmosphere. Each step requires specific microbes, making the cycle multi-stepped and easy to disrupt.

**What is biomagnification?**
Biomagnification is the increasing concentration of a pollutant in organisms as it moves up the food chain. Toxins like DDT and mercury are stored in body tissues and not easily broken down. A predator eats many prey, so the predator accumulates a much higher concentration than any single prey item. This is why apex predators (eagles, sharks, large fish) often have dangerously high toxin levels.

**Are humans causing the 6th mass extinction?**
Many ecologists argue yes. Current extinction rates are estimated to be 100–1,000 times the natural background rate, driven by habitat destruction, climate change, invasive species, pollution, and overexploitation. Whether this technically qualifies as a mass extinction depends on how it's measured, but the rate of biodiversity loss is unprecedented in geological terms — and unlike past mass extinctions, this one is caused by a single species.
