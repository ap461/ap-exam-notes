## Key concepts to master

### Cell theory

The three core ideas of modern cell theory:

1. All living things are made of one or more cells
2. The cell is the basic unit of life
3. All cells come from pre-existing cells

---

### Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes

| Feature | Prokaryotes | Eukaryotes |
|---|---|---|
| **Nucleus** | No (DNA in nucleoid region) | Yes |
| **Membrane-bound organelles** | No | Yes |
| **Size** | Smaller (1–10 μm) | Larger (10–100 μm) |
| **DNA** | Circular, in cytoplasm | Linear, in nucleus |
| **Ribosomes** | Smaller (70S) | Larger (80S) |
| **Cell wall** | Yes (peptidoglycan in bacteria) | Plants and fungi yes; animals no |
| **Examples** | Bacteria, archaea | Animals, plants, fungi, protists |

---

### Why cells stay small (surface area to volume ratio)

As a cell gets bigger, **volume grows faster than surface area**. This becomes a problem because:

- The cell needs surface area to take in nutrients and remove waste
- The cell's volume is what needs supplying

If a cell gets too big, the surface area can't keep up with the metabolic needs of the volume. This is why cells stay small — and why larger organisms have many cells, not bigger cells.

**Key formula relationships:**
- Surface area scales with **r²** (radius squared)
- Volume scales with **r³** (radius cubed)
- As radius increases, volume outpaces surface area

## Organelles (must know!)

### In all eukaryotic cells

| Organelle | Function |
|---|---|
| **Nucleus** | Stores DNA, controls gene expression, site of transcription |
| **Nucleolus** | Inside the nucleus; makes ribosomes |
| **Ribosomes** | Site of protein synthesis (translation); free or attached to rough ER |
| **Rough ER** | Studded with ribosomes; modifies and folds proteins |
| **Smooth ER** | Synthesizes lipids; detoxifies drugs; stores calcium |
| **Golgi apparatus** | Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for shipping |
| **Mitochondria** | Cellular respiration; produces ATP; has its own DNA |
| **Lysosomes** | Contain digestive enzymes; break down waste and damaged organelles |
| **Peroxisomes** | Break down fatty acids; detoxify alcohol; produce hydrogen peroxide |
| **Cytoskeleton** | Microtubules, microfilaments, intermediate filaments — provide shape and support |
| **Plasma membrane** | Selectively permeable barrier; controls what enters and leaves |
| **Cytoplasm** | Fluid filling the cell; site of many reactions |

### In plant cells only

| Organelle | Function |
|---|---|
| **Chloroplast** | Site of photosynthesis; has its own DNA |
| **Cell wall** | Made of cellulose; provides structure and protection |
| **Central vacuole** | Stores water, ions, pigments; provides turgor pressure |
| **Plasmodesmata** | Channels through cell walls connecting plant cells |

### In animal cells only

- **Centrioles** — organize microtubules during cell division
- **Lysosomes** — abundant in animal cells (less common in plants)

## The endomembrane system

A network of organelles that work together to **make, modify, and transport proteins and lipids**.

**Order of protein flow:**

1. **Nucleus** — DNA → mRNA
2. **Ribosome (on rough ER)** — mRNA → protein
3. **Rough ER** — folds and modifies the protein
4. **Vesicle** — buds off ER and travels to Golgi
5. **Golgi apparatus** — further modifies, sorts, packages
6. **Vesicle** — buds off Golgi
7. **Plasma membrane** — protein either secreted or embedded

---

## Endosymbiotic theory

Mitochondria and chloroplasts evolved from **free-living prokaryotes** that were engulfed by a larger cell about 2 billion years ago.

**Evidence:**
- Both have their own circular DNA (like bacteria)
- Both have 70S ribosomes (like prokaryotes)
- Both have double membranes (the inner membrane is the original prokaryote's, the outer is the engulfing cell's)
- Both reproduce by binary fission, independently of the cell

## The plasma membrane (fluid mosaic model)

The membrane is a **fluid bilayer of phospholipids** with proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates floating in it.

### Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| **Phospholipid bilayer** | Hydrophilic heads outside, hydrophobic tails inside |
| **Cholesterol** | Stabilizes membrane fluidity (less fluid in heat, more fluid in cold) |
| **Integral (transmembrane) proteins** | Span the membrane; transport, signaling |
| **Peripheral proteins** | On the surface of the membrane; signaling, structure |
| **Glycoproteins / glycolipids** | Carbohydrates on outer surface; cell-cell recognition |

### Selective permeability

The membrane lets some things through and blocks others:

- **Easily cross:** small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂), small polar molecules (H₂O — slowly)
- **Cannot cross alone:** large polar molecules (glucose), ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻), large molecules (proteins)

---

## Membrane transport

### Passive transport (no ATP needed)

Molecules move **down their concentration gradient** (high → low).

| Type | What moves | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple diffusion** | Small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) | Directly through the bilayer |
| **Facilitated diffusion** | Polar molecules, ions | Through channel or carrier proteins |
| **Osmosis** | Water | Through aquaporins or directly through bilayer |

### Active transport (requires ATP)

Molecules move **against their gradient** (low → high).

| Type | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary active transport** | Uses ATP directly | Sodium-potassium pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in) |
| **Secondary active transport** | Uses an existing gradient as energy | Glucose transport in intestines |
| **Endocytosis** | Cell takes in materials by engulfing them | Phagocytosis (solids), pinocytosis (liquids), receptor-mediated endocytosis |
| **Exocytosis** | Cell releases materials by fusing vesicle with membrane | Hormone secretion, neurotransmitter release |

## Tonicity (osmosis in different solutions)

The relative concentration of **solutes outside vs inside** the cell determines water movement.

| Solution | Solute concentration | Water moves | Animal cell | Plant cell |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypotonic** | Lower outside than inside | Into the cell | Lyses (bursts) | Becomes turgid (firm) — ideal |
| **Isotonic** | Equal | No net movement | Normal | Flaccid (limp) |
| **Hypertonic** | Higher outside than inside | Out of the cell | Shrivels (crenation) | Plasmolysis (membrane pulls away from wall) |

**Memory tip:** water moves toward the side with **more solutes** (because that side has less free water).

---

## Water potential (Ψ)

Predicts the direction water will move. Water moves from **high water potential to low water potential**.

**Formula:** Ψ = Ψₛ + Ψₚ

- **Ψₛ (solute potential)** — always negative or zero; more solute = more negative
- **Ψₚ (pressure potential)** — usually positive in plants (turgor pressure)

**Pure water at atmospheric pressure** has Ψ = 0. Adding solutes makes it negative; adding pressure makes it positive.

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Calling all small molecules "free to cross" the membrane** — only small nonpolar molecules cross easily; even water needs aquaporins to cross quickly
- **Confusing facilitated diffusion with active transport** — both use proteins, but only active transport requires ATP
- **Mixing up hypotonic and hypertonic** — hypo = less solute outside, water rushes in; hyper = more solute outside, water rushes out
- **Forgetting plant vs animal cell tonicity outcomes** — plants in hypotonic = turgid (good); animals in hypotonic = burst (bad)
- **Saying mitochondria and chloroplasts make their own ATP for the cell** — they generate ATP, but mitochondria do it for the whole cell; chloroplasts use it for the Calvin cycle
- **Confusing rough ER with smooth ER** — rough = ribosomes, makes proteins; smooth = no ribosomes, makes lipids
- **Treating the cytoskeleton as one structure** — it includes microtubules (largest), microfilaments (smallest, actin), and intermediate filaments

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 2?**
Unit 2 makes up about 10–13% of multiple-choice questions and is a frequent FRQ topic. Membrane structure, transport, and tonicity are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between simple diffusion and facilitated diffusion?**
Simple diffusion is when small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) pass directly through the phospholipid bilayer. Facilitated diffusion is when polar molecules or ions pass through channel or carrier proteins — they still move down their concentration gradient (no ATP), they just need help getting through the membrane.

**Why do cells need to be small?**
As a cell grows, its volume increases faster than its surface area. The cell membrane (surface area) is responsible for taking in nutrients and removing waste, while the cell's interior (volume) needs supplying. If the cell gets too big, the membrane can't keep up with the demand. Cells solve this by staying small or developing folds (microvilli) and projections to increase surface area.

**What is the fluid mosaic model?**
The fluid mosaic model describes the plasma membrane as a fluid lipid bilayer with various proteins floating like icebergs in a sea. "Fluid" because the lipids and proteins move laterally; "mosaic" because the membrane has many different components arranged like tiles in a mosaic.

**What happens to a red blood cell in pure water?**
Pure water is hypotonic compared to the cell's interior, so water rushes into the cell by osmosis. The cell swells and eventually bursts (lysis) because animal cells have no cell wall to resist the pressure. This is why IV fluids are isotonic — to prevent damage to red blood cells.

**What is endosymbiotic theory and why is it important?**
Endosymbiotic theory says mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living prokaryotes that were engulfed by a larger cell. Over time, they became permanent residents and lost the ability to live independently. Evidence includes their own DNA, 70S ribosomes, double membranes, and binary fission reproduction. The theory explains a major step in eukaryotic evolution.

**Do plant cells have lysosomes?**
Plant cells generally don't have traditional lysosomes. Instead, the central vacuole performs many similar functions — including breaking down waste and storing degradative enzymes. Animal cells rely on lysosomes more heavily.
