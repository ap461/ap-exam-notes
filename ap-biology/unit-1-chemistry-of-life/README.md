# AP Biology Unit 1: Chemistry of Life

> The chemical basis of life — water, macromolecules, and how molecular structure determines function. This unit covers the unique properties of water, the four major macromolecules, and how monomers join to form polymers.

## What is Unit 1 about?

Unit 1 of AP Biology lays the chemical foundation for the rest of the course. You'll learn why water is essential for life, how the four major macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) are built from monomers, and how the structure of a molecule determines its function. This unit makes up about 8–11% of the exam — the smallest by exam weight — but every later unit builds on it. You can't understand cell membranes (Unit 2), enzymes (Unit 3), DNA (Unit 6), or natural selection (Unit 7) without first understanding the chemistry here.

## Key concepts to master

### The elements of life

Living things are mostly made of just **6 elements**: carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and sulfur (S) — often abbreviated **CHNOPS**.

| Element | Where it shows up |
|---|---|
| **Carbon** | Backbone of all organic molecules |
| **Hydrogen** | In water and all organic molecules |
| **Oxygen** | Water, sugars, cellular respiration |
| **Nitrogen** | Proteins, nucleic acids |
| **Phosphorus** | DNA backbone, ATP, phospholipids |
| **Sulfur** | Some amino acids (cysteine, methionine), disulfide bridges |

### Water and its properties

Water is essential for life because of its unique properties — all caused by **hydrogen bonding** between polar water molecules.

| Property | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Polarity** | Uneven distribution of charge (O is δ−, H is δ+) | Allows hydrogen bonding |
| **Cohesion** | Water sticks to itself | Surface tension; water moves up plant stems |
| **Adhesion** | Water sticks to other surfaces | Capillary action in plants |
| **High specific heat** | Water resists temperature change | Stabilizes climates and body temperature |
| **High heat of vaporization** | Takes lots of energy to evaporate | Sweating cools the body |
| **Less dense as solid** | Ice floats | Insulates lakes; aquatic life survives winter |
| **Universal solvent** | Dissolves polar and ionic substances | Enables most cellular reactions |

### Acids, bases, and pH

- **Acid** — releases H⁺ (low pH, < 7)
- **Base** — accepts H⁺ or releases OH⁻ (high pH, > 7)
- **pH scale** — 0 (acidic) to 14 (basic), 7 is neutral
- **Each pH unit = 10× change** in H⁺ concentration
- **Buffers** — resist pH changes by accepting or donating H⁺ (essential for blood and cells)

## The four macromolecules

All four are built from smaller monomers joined by **dehydration synthesis** (removing water) and broken apart by **hydrolysis** (adding water).

### 1. Carbohydrates

**Function:** energy storage and structural support
**Monomer:** monosaccharide (e.g., glucose)
**Polymer:** polysaccharide

| Type | Examples | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Monosaccharides** | Glucose, fructose, galactose | Quick energy |
| **Disaccharides** | Sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose, maltose | Transport sugars |
| **Polysaccharides — storage** | Starch (plants), glycogen (animals) | Store energy |
| **Polysaccharides — structural** | Cellulose (plant cell walls), chitin (fungi, insect exoskeletons) | Provide structure |

### 2. Lipids

**Function:** long-term energy storage, membranes, signaling
**Not built from a single monomer** (the only macromolecule without a true monomer)

| Type | Structure | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Triglycerides (fats/oils)** | Glycerol + 3 fatty acids | Long-term energy storage |
| **Phospholipids** | Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group | Cell membranes (form bilayer) |
| **Steroids** | Four fused carbon rings | Cholesterol, sex hormones |
| **Waxes** | Fatty acids + alcohols | Waterproof coatings on plants and animals |

**Saturated vs unsaturated:**
- **Saturated** — no double bonds, straight chains, solid at room temp (butter, lard)
- **Unsaturated** — one or more double bonds, kinked chains, liquid at room temp (olive oil, fish oil)

### 3. Proteins

**Function:** almost everything — enzymes, transport, structure, signaling, immune response
**Monomer:** amino acid (20 different ones)
**Polymer:** polypeptide / protein

**Amino acid structure:**
- Central carbon
- Amino group (−NH₂)
- Carboxyl group (−COOH)
- Hydrogen
- **R group (side chain)** — determines the amino acid's properties

**Peptide bond** — links amino acids together via dehydration synthesis.

### Levels of protein structure

| Level | What it is | Bonds involved |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary (1°)** | Linear sequence of amino acids | Peptide bonds |
| **Secondary (2°)** | Local folding into α-helix or β-pleated sheet | Hydrogen bonds (between backbone) |
| **Tertiary (3°)** | Overall 3D shape of one polypeptide | R-group interactions: hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges, hydrophobic interactions |
| **Quaternary (4°)** | Multiple polypeptides combined | Same R-group interactions, between subunits |

**Denaturation** — heat, pH change, or chemicals disrupt the bonds → protein loses shape → loses function. Primary structure is preserved (peptide bonds are not broken).

### 4. Nucleic acids

**Function:** store and transmit genetic information
**Monomer:** nucleotide
**Polymer:** DNA or RNA

**Nucleotide structure:**
- 5-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA)
- Phosphate group
- Nitrogenous base

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---|---|---|
| **Sugar** | Deoxyribose | Ribose |
| **Strands** | Double-stranded | Usually single-stranded |
| **Bases** | A, T, G, C | A, U, G, C (uracil instead of thymine) |
| **Function** | Long-term storage of genetic info | Protein synthesis (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA) |

**Base pairing in DNA:** A pairs with T (2 H-bonds); G pairs with C (3 H-bonds). G-C bonds are stronger.
**In RNA:** A pairs with U.

**Antiparallel strands:** DNA's two strands run in opposite directions (5' to 3' on one, 3' to 5' on the other).

## Dehydration synthesis vs hydrolysis

| Process | What happens | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Dehydration synthesis (condensation)** | Joins monomers; removes a water molecule | Builds polymers |
| **Hydrolysis** | Breaks polymers; adds a water molecule | Breaks down polymers |

**Memory tip:** **De**hydration removes water; **Hydro**lysis adds water (lysis = "to break").

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing the four macromolecules** — know the monomer, polymer, function, and one example for each
- **Mixing up dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis** — dehydration **removes** water; hydrolysis **adds** water
- **Forgetting that lipids don't have a true monomer** — fatty acids are *not* monomers in the same way amino acids are
- **Calling cellulose digestible** — humans cannot digest cellulose (we lack the enzyme); it acts as fiber
- **Confusing primary and secondary structure** — primary = sequence; secondary = local folding (helix or sheet)
- **Saying denaturation breaks peptide bonds** — denaturation breaks weaker bonds (H-bonds, ionic, hydrophobic). Peptide bonds (primary structure) usually remain intact
- **Forgetting that DNA strands are antiparallel** — this matters for replication and transcription

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 1?**
Unit 1 makes up about 8–11% of multiple-choice questions — the smallest unit by weight. However, the chemistry concepts here support every other unit, so weak Unit 1 knowledge will hurt you across the entire exam.

**What's the difference between cohesion and adhesion?**
Cohesion is water sticking to itself (creates surface tension, lets insects walk on water). Adhesion is water sticking to other surfaces (creates capillary action, helps water climb up plant xylem). Both result from hydrogen bonding.

**Why is water polar?**
Oxygen has a stronger pull on shared electrons than hydrogen does (it's more electronegative). This creates a partial negative charge near oxygen and partial positive charges near the hydrogens. The uneven distribution makes water polar — and polarity is the reason for almost all its life-supporting properties.

**What does it mean that DNA strands are antiparallel?**
The two strands run in opposite directions. One strand goes 5' to 3', the other goes 3' to 5'. This matters because DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end during replication — leading to leading and lagging strand differences.

**Why are saturated fats solid at room temperature?**
Saturated fats have no double bonds, so the fatty acid chains are straight and pack tightly together. Tight packing creates stronger intermolecular forces and a higher melting point — solid at room temp. Unsaturated fats have kinks (from double bonds), so they pack loosely and stay liquid at room temp.

**What happens when a protein is denatured?**
Heat, pH change, or chemicals break the weaker bonds holding the protein in shape (hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges, hydrophobic interactions). The protein unfolds and loses its function. The primary structure (peptide bonds) usually stays intact, so denaturation isn't the same as breaking the protein into pieces.

**Why does ice float?**
In liquid water, hydrogen bonds constantly form and break. In ice, water molecules form a stable crystalline lattice with each molecule held at a fixed distance by hydrogen bonds. This lattice spaces molecules farther apart than in liquid water, so ice is less dense — and floats. This property insulates lakes and oceans, allowing aquatic life to survive winter.
