## What is Unit 3 about?

Unit 3 of AP Biology studies how cells process energy. You'll learn how enzymes lower activation energy to drive reactions, how plants capture light energy and convert it to chemical energy through photosynthesis, and how all cells extract energy from glucose through cellular respiration. This unit makes up about 12–16% of the exam — one of the highest-weight units — and is the most diagram-heavy unit. Expect to interpret graphs of enzyme activity, label the parts of a chloroplast and mitochondrion, and trace electrons through the photosynthesis and respiration pathways.

## Key concepts to master

### Energy and the laws of thermodynamics

| Law | What it says | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **First law** | Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed | Cells convert chemical energy → kinetic energy → heat |
| **Second law** | Every energy transfer increases entropy (disorder) | No reaction is 100% efficient — some energy is lost as heat |

### Free energy (ΔG)

| Term | What it means | Reaction direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Exergonic (−ΔG)** | Releases free energy | Spontaneous; happens on its own |
| **Endergonic (+ΔG)** | Requires free energy input | Not spontaneous; needs energy |
| **Coupled reactions** | Exergonic powers endergonic | ATP hydrolysis powers most cellular work |

### ATP — the energy currency

**Structure:** adenine + ribose + 3 phosphate groups
**Energy storage:** in the bonds between phosphates
**ATP → ADP + Pi** releases energy (exergonic, used by cells)
**ADP + Pi → ATP** stores energy (endergonic, requires energy from food or light)

## Enzymes

**Enzymes** are biological catalysts (almost always proteins) that **lower activation energy** so reactions happen faster.

### Key enzyme concepts

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Active site** | The pocket where the substrate binds |
| **Substrate** | The molecule the enzyme acts on |
| **Induced fit** | The active site changes shape slightly to fit the substrate (not lock-and-key) |
| **Specificity** | One enzyme = one type of reaction (shape determines function) |
| **Activation energy** | Energy needed to start a reaction; enzymes lower this |
| **Reusable** | Enzymes are not consumed in the reaction |

### Factors that affect enzyme activity

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Temperature** | Each enzyme has an optimal temp; too hot = denatures; too cold = slow |
| **pH** | Each enzyme has an optimal pH; too far off = denatures (e.g., pepsin at pH 2, trypsin at pH 8) |
| **Substrate concentration** | More substrate = faster, until enzymes saturate (V max) |
| **Enzyme concentration** | More enzymes = faster (if substrate is plentiful) |
| **Inhibitors** | Slow or stop enzyme activity |

### Types of inhibition

| Type | How it works |
|---|---|
| **Competitive** | Inhibitor binds to active site, blocks substrate; can be overcome with more substrate |
| **Noncompetitive (allosteric)** | Inhibitor binds elsewhere, changes enzyme shape; cannot be overcome with more substrate |
| **Allosteric activation** | A regulator binds elsewhere and increases activity |
| **Feedback inhibition** | The end product inhibits an early enzyme in the pathway (prevents waste) |

## Photosynthesis

The process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria capture light energy and convert it into chemical energy stored in glucose.

**Overall equation:**
**6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂**

**Two stages:**
1. **Light-dependent reactions** (in thylakoid membrane)
2. **Calvin cycle / light-independent reactions** (in stroma)

### Chloroplast structure

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| **Outer membrane** | Boundary of the chloroplast |
| **Inner membrane** | Inner boundary |
| **Stroma** | Fluid surrounding the thylakoids; site of the Calvin cycle |
| **Thylakoids** | Disc-shaped membranes; site of light-dependent reactions |
| **Grana** | Stacks of thylakoids |
| **Chlorophyll** | Pigment in thylakoid membrane that absorbs light (reflects green) |

### Light-dependent reactions

**Location:** thylakoid membrane
**Inputs:** light, H₂O, NADP⁺, ADP
**Outputs:** O₂, NADPH, ATP

**The basic flow:**

1. Light hits **Photosystem II** → excites electrons
2. Water is split (photolysis): H₂O → 2H⁺ + ½O₂ + 2e⁻ (this is where O₂ comes from!)
3. Electrons travel through the **electron transport chain**, pumping H⁺ into the thylakoid
4. Electrons reach **Photosystem I**, get re-energized by light
5. Electrons reduce **NADP⁺ → NADPH**
6. H⁺ flows back through **ATP synthase**, making ATP (chemiosmosis)

### Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions)

**Location:** stroma
**Inputs:** CO₂, ATP, NADPH
**Outputs:** glucose (G3P), ADP, NADP⁺

**Three phases:**

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **1. Carbon fixation** | CO₂ + RuBP → 3-PGA (catalyzed by **RuBisCO**) |
| **2. Reduction** | 3-PGA + ATP + NADPH → G3P |
| **3. Regeneration** | Most G3P regenerates RuBP; some becomes glucose |

**Key fact:** It takes **6 turns of the Calvin cycle** to make one glucose molecule.

### C3, C4, and CAM plants

Plants in hot, dry climates evolved different strategies to deal with photorespiration (when RuBisCO grabs O₂ instead of CO₂).

| Type | Where | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **C3** | Most plants, cool/wet climates | Standard Calvin cycle; CO₂ enters mesophyll cells directly |
| **C4** | Hot, sunny climates (corn, sugarcane) | CO₂ fixed in mesophyll, then transferred to bundle sheath cells |
| **CAM** | Desert plants (cacti, succulents) | Open stomata at night to collect CO₂; do Calvin cycle during the day |

## Cellular respiration

The process all cells use to break down glucose and produce ATP.

**Overall equation:**
**C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + ATP (~30–32)**

**Four stages:**
1. Glycolysis
2. Pyruvate oxidation
3. Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)
4. Oxidative phosphorylation (ETC + chemiosmosis)

### Mitochondrion structure

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| **Outer membrane** | Boundary |
| **Inner membrane** | Folded into cristae; site of ETC and ATP synthase |
| **Cristae** | Folds that increase surface area |
| **Matrix** | Inner fluid; site of Krebs cycle |
| **Intermembrane space** | Between membranes; H⁺ accumulates here |

### Stage 1: Glycolysis

**Location:** cytoplasm
**Inputs:** glucose, 2 ATP, 2 NAD⁺
**Outputs:** 2 pyruvate, **net 2 ATP**, 2 NADH

**Key facts:**
- Happens in all organisms (anaerobic — no O₂ needed)
- Glucose (6C) → 2 pyruvate (3C each)
- Investment phase uses 2 ATP; payoff phase makes 4 ATP (net = 2)

### Stage 2: Pyruvate oxidation

**Location:** mitochondrial matrix (eukaryotes)
**Inputs:** 2 pyruvate, 2 NAD⁺, 2 CoA
**Outputs:** 2 acetyl-CoA, **2 CO₂**, 2 NADH

### Stage 3: Krebs cycle (citric acid cycle)

**Location:** mitochondrial matrix
**Inputs (per glucose):** 2 acetyl-CoA, ADP, NAD⁺, FAD
**Outputs (per glucose):** **4 CO₂**, **2 ATP**, **6 NADH**, **2 FADH₂**

**Key facts:**
- Cycle runs **twice per glucose** (once per acetyl-CoA)
- All 6 carbons of glucose are released as CO₂ by the end of the Krebs cycle
- Generates lots of NADH and FADH₂ — these carry electrons to the ETC

### Stage 4: Oxidative phosphorylation (ETC + chemiosmosis)

**Location:** inner mitochondrial membrane
**Inputs:** NADH, FADH₂, O₂, ADP
**Outputs:** **~26–28 ATP**, H₂O, NAD⁺, FAD

**The basic flow:**

1. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the **electron transport chain**
2. Electrons pass through proteins, releasing energy
3. Energy pumps H⁺ from matrix into intermembrane space
4. H⁺ accumulates, creating a gradient (proton motive force)
5. H⁺ flows back through **ATP synthase** → makes ATP (chemiosmosis)
6. **O₂ is the final electron acceptor**: 4e⁻ + 4H⁺ + O₂ → 2 H₂O

**Why O₂ matters:** without O₂ to accept electrons at the end, the entire ETC backs up and stops. This is why we need to breathe.

### Total ATP yield per glucose

| Stage | ATP made |
|---|---|
| Glycolysis | 2 ATP |
| Krebs cycle | 2 ATP |
| Oxidative phosphorylation | ~26–28 ATP |
| **Total** | **~30–32 ATP** |

## Fermentation (anaerobic)

When O₂ is **not** available, cells can't run the ETC. They use fermentation to keep glycolysis going by regenerating NAD⁺.

| Type | Where | Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Lactic acid fermentation** | Animal muscle cells, some bacteria | Lactic acid + NAD⁺ |
| **Alcohol fermentation** | Yeast, some bacteria | Ethanol + CO₂ + NAD⁺ |

**Key fact:** fermentation only produces 2 ATP per glucose (from glycolysis) — much less than aerobic respiration's ~30–32.

## Comparing photosynthesis and respiration

| Feature | Photosynthesis | Respiration |
|---|---|---|
| **Where** | Chloroplasts (plants only) | Mitochondria (all eukaryotes) |
| **Input** | CO₂ + H₂O + light | C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂ |
| **Output** | C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂ | CO₂ + H₂O + ATP |
| **Energy** | Stores energy in glucose | Releases energy from glucose |
| **Electron flow** | Water → NADPH (uphill) | Glucose → O₂ (downhill) |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Saying photosynthesis is the opposite of respiration** — they aren't perfectly reversed, but the products of one are inputs of the other
- **Confusing the locations** — light reactions in **thylakoid membrane**; Calvin cycle in **stroma**; Krebs in **matrix**; ETC on **inner mitochondrial membrane**
- **Calling RuBisCO an enzyme of glycolysis** — RuBisCO is the Calvin cycle enzyme that fixes CO₂
- **Forgetting that water is split in photosynthesis** — that's where O₂ comes from
- **Forgetting that O₂ is the final electron acceptor in respiration** — without it, the ETC stops
- **Mixing up ATP yield** — glycolysis (2) + Krebs (2) + oxidative phosphorylation (~26–28) = ~30–32 total
- **Treating fermentation as ATP-producing** — fermentation **doesn't make ATP**; it just regenerates NAD⁺ so glycolysis can keep making ATP
- **Confusing competitive and noncompetitive inhibition** — competitive can be overcome with more substrate; noncompetitive cannot

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 3?**
Unit 3 makes up about 12–16% of multiple-choice questions and is one of the most-tested units on FRQs. Photosynthesis, cellular respiration, and enzyme function are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between competitive and noncompetitive inhibition?**
Competitive inhibitors bind to the enzyme's active site and physically block the substrate. Adding more substrate can overcome this. Noncompetitive (allosteric) inhibitors bind to a different site and change the enzyme's shape, so the active site no longer fits the substrate. Adding more substrate cannot overcome noncompetitive inhibition.

**Where does the oxygen we breathe out come from?**
Surprisingly, the oxygen plants release during photosynthesis comes from **water** (H₂O), not CO₂. In the light-dependent reactions, water is split (photolysis), releasing electrons, hydrogen ions, and oxygen.

**Why do we need oxygen?**
Oxygen is the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain. Without O₂ to accept electrons at the end, the entire ETC backs up and stops, which means no ATP is produced through oxidative phosphorylation. Without ATP, cells can't function — and we die within minutes.

**What's the difference between substrate-level and oxidative phosphorylation?**
Substrate-level phosphorylation makes ATP directly by transferring a phosphate from a substrate to ADP (happens in glycolysis and Krebs cycle). Oxidative phosphorylation makes ATP indirectly using the H⁺ gradient created by the ETC powering ATP synthase. Most ATP comes from oxidative phosphorylation.

**Why is fermentation important if it makes so little ATP?**
Fermentation doesn't actually make ATP itself. It regenerates NAD⁺ so glycolysis can keep producing 2 ATP per glucose. Without fermentation (or aerobic respiration), NAD⁺ runs out and glycolysis stops. So fermentation is a "lifeline" that keeps glycolysis going when O₂ isn't available.

**Why does temperature affect enzyme activity?**
At low temperatures, molecules move slowly, so enzyme-substrate collisions are rare and reactions are slow. As temperature rises, collisions increase and reactions speed up — until temperatures get too high, at which point the enzyme **denatures** (loses its shape) and can no longer bind the substrate. Each enzyme has an optimal temperature where it works best.

**What is chemiosmosis?**
Chemiosmosis is the process where energy stored in a hydrogen ion (H⁺) gradient powers ATP synthesis. The ETC pumps H⁺ across a membrane, building up a gradient. When H⁺ flows back across the membrane through ATP synthase, the energy of that flow drives ATP production. This happens in both photosynthesis (across the thylakoid membrane) and cellular respiration (across the inner mitochondrial membrane).
