## What is Unit 4 about?

Unit 4 of AP Biology covers two big topics: how cells communicate with each other, and how cells divide and regulate their division. You'll learn how a signal travels from outside a cell to trigger a response inside, how the body uses feedback loops to maintain homeostasis, the phases of the cell cycle, the steps of mitosis, and what happens when cell cycle control breaks down (cancer). This unit makes up about 10–15% of the exam and is heavy on flow diagrams — expect to trace signal pathways and identify cell cycle phases from images.

## Key concepts to master

### Why cells communicate

Cells communicate to coordinate behavior, respond to environment, and maintain homeostasis. Communication is essential for:

- Multicellular development
- Immune response
- Hormonal regulation
- Growth and repair
- Reproduction

## Types of cell signaling

| Type | Distance | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct contact** | Cells touching | Plasmodesmata in plants, gap junctions in animals, immune cell recognition |
| **Local signaling — paracrine** | Short distance | Growth factors, neurotransmitters between nearby cells |
| **Local signaling — synaptic** | Across a synapse | Neurons signaling to neurons or muscle cells |
| **Long-distance — endocrine (hormonal)** | Throughout the body | Hormones traveling in bloodstream (insulin, estrogen) |
| **Autocrine** | Cell signals itself | Some immune cells, cancer cells |

## The 3 stages of signal transduction

A signal molecule (ligand) outside a cell triggers a response inside through three stages:

### Stage 1: Reception

A **ligand** (signal molecule) binds to a **receptor**. The receptor changes shape.

| Receptor type | Where | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR)** | Plasma membrane | Activates G-protein, triggers cascade |
| **Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK)** | Plasma membrane | Adds phosphates to proteins, can activate multiple pathways at once |
| **Ligand-gated ion channel** | Plasma membrane | Opens to let specific ions through (e.g., neurotransmitter receptors) |
| **Intracellular receptor** | Inside the cell (cytoplasm or nucleus) | Used by hydrophobic signals (steroid hormones) that cross the membrane |

### Stage 2: Transduction

A **signal cascade** relays and amplifies the signal inside the cell.

| Concept | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Phosphorylation cascade** | Series of protein kinases adding phosphates, activating each next protein |
| **Second messengers** | Small molecules that spread the signal (cAMP, calcium ions Ca²⁺) |
| **Amplification** | Each step activates many of the next, so one signal triggers a huge response |

### Stage 3: Response

The signal triggers a **specific cellular outcome**:

- Activate or inhibit an enzyme
- Open or close ion channels
- Change gene expression (turn genes on/off)
- Trigger cell division, growth, or apoptosis

## Feedback loops

Feedback loops keep biological systems in a stable range (homeostasis).

| Type | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Negative feedback** | The output **reduces** the original signal — keeps things stable | Body temperature regulation, blood glucose levels, blood pressure |
| **Positive feedback** | The output **amplifies** the original signal — drives toward an endpoint | Childbirth contractions, blood clotting, action potentials |

**Most biological feedback is negative** — it maintains balance. Positive feedback is rarer and used when a process needs to go to completion quickly.

---

## Apoptosis (programmed cell death)

A cell receives a signal that triggers its own controlled death.

**Why it matters:**
- Removes damaged or infected cells
- Sculpts tissues during development (e.g., separating fingers in embryos)
- Prevents cancer
- Maintains immune balance

**When apoptosis fails:** cells that should die survive — leading to cancer, autoimmune disease, or developmental defects.

## The cell cycle

The complete sequence a cell goes through from one division to the next. Most of the time, cells are in **interphase** (~90% of the cycle); the rest is **mitosis (M phase)**.

### Phases of the cell cycle

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **G1 (Gap 1)** | Cell grows, makes proteins and organelles, performs normal functions |
| **S (Synthesis)** | DNA replicates — each chromosome now has 2 sister chromatids |
| **G2 (Gap 2)** | Cell continues to grow, prepares for division |
| **M (Mitosis)** | Cell divides nucleus (mitosis) and cytoplasm (cytokinesis) |
| **G0** | Resting phase; cell exits the cycle (e.g., neurons, mature muscle cells) |

---

## Mitosis (the 4 phases)

Mitosis divides one cell's nucleus into two genetically identical nuclei.

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Prophase** | Chromosomes condense and become visible; nuclear envelope breaks down; spindle fibers form |
| **Metaphase** | Chromosomes line up along the metaphase plate (cell equator) |
| **Anaphase** | Sister chromatids separate; pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers |
| **Telophase** | New nuclear envelopes form around each set; chromosomes start to decondense |
| **Cytokinesis** | Cytoplasm divides — animal cells form a cleavage furrow; plant cells form a cell plate |

**Memory tip:** **PMAT** = **P**rophase, **M**etaphase, **A**naphase, **T**elophase.

**Result:** 1 parent cell → 2 identical diploid daughter cells.

### Mitosis in animal vs plant cells

| Feature | Animal | Plant |
|---|---|---|
| **Centrosomes/centrioles** | Yes | No (still forms spindle, but no centrioles) |
| **Cytokinesis** | Cleavage furrow pinches inward | Cell plate forms in the middle |
| **Cell wall** | Absent | Builds new wall during cytokinesis |

## Cell cycle regulation

The cell cycle is tightly controlled by **cyclins** and **cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs)**.

| Concept | What to know |
|---|---|
| **Cyclins** | Proteins that rise and fall throughout the cycle |
| **CDKs (cyclin-dependent kinases)** | Activated when bound to cyclins; phosphorylate target proteins |
| **Cyclin-CDK complex** | Drives the cell forward at each checkpoint |
| **Checkpoints** | Stop points where the cell verifies conditions before continuing |

### The 3 main checkpoints

| Checkpoint | When | What it checks |
|---|---|---|
| **G1 checkpoint (restriction point)** | End of G1 | Cell size, nutrients, growth signals, DNA damage. If passed, cell commits to dividing |
| **G2 checkpoint** | End of G2 | DNA fully replicated and undamaged; cell ready for mitosis |
| **M (spindle) checkpoint** | During metaphase | All chromosomes attached to spindle fibers before anaphase begins |

## Cancer (cell cycle gone wrong)

Cancer happens when cells **divide uncontrollably** because cell cycle regulation has failed.

### Two key gene types involved

| Gene type | Normal function | What happens in cancer |
|---|---|---|
| **Proto-oncogenes** | Promote cell division (gas pedal) | Mutate into oncogenes — stuck "on" → too much division |
| **Tumor suppressor genes** | Stop division when needed (brake pedal) | Lose function → no brakes → uncontrolled division |

**Famous examples:**
- **p53** — a tumor suppressor that triggers DNA repair or apoptosis when DNA is damaged. Mutated in over 50% of cancers.
- **Ras** — a proto-oncogene. Mutations make it permanently "on," driving constant cell division.

### Hallmarks of cancer cells

- Ignore cell cycle checkpoints
- Avoid apoptosis (don't die when they should)
- Ignore stop signals from neighboring cells (no contact inhibition)
- Promote their own blood supply (angiogenesis)
- Can spread to other tissues (metastasis)

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Confusing reception, transduction, and response** — reception = ligand binds receptor; transduction = relay inside cell; response = the actual change
- **Mixing up positive and negative feedback** — negative feedback **reduces** the signal (most common); positive feedback **amplifies** it (rare)
- **Saying mitosis produces gametes** — mitosis produces somatic (body) cells; **meiosis** (Unit 5) produces gametes
- **Forgetting that DNA replicates in S phase, not M phase** — by the time mitosis starts, DNA is already duplicated
- **Confusing chromosome and chromatid** — after S phase, each chromosome has 2 sister chromatids; they separate in anaphase
- **Saying tumor suppressor genes "cause cancer"** — they cause cancer when **lost**, not when present (they normally prevent it)
- **Confusing GPCRs and RTKs** — both are membrane receptors, but GPCRs activate G-proteins; RTKs phosphorylate proteins directly

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 4?**
Unit 4 makes up about 10–15% of multiple-choice questions and frequently appears in FRQs. Cell signaling pathways, feedback loops, mitosis, and cancer are the most-tested concepts.

**What's the difference between negative and positive feedback?**
Negative feedback works like a thermostat — when the output gets too high, the system reduces production to bring it back to normal. Positive feedback amplifies the original signal — once started, it drives the process to completion. Most biological systems use negative feedback (temperature, blood sugar, blood pressure). Positive feedback is rarer and used when a process needs to go fast and finish (childbirth, blood clotting, action potentials).

**What's the difference between mitosis and meiosis?**
Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells from one parent cell. It's used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction. Meiosis (covered in Unit 5) produces four genetically different haploid gametes — the cells used in sexual reproduction. Mitosis = one division, meiosis = two divisions.

**Why is p53 called the "guardian of the genome"?**
p53 is a tumor suppressor protein that monitors DNA damage. When DNA is damaged, p53 stops the cell cycle to allow repair, or — if the damage is too severe — triggers apoptosis to kill the cell. Without functional p53, damaged cells continue dividing and accumulate mutations, leading to cancer. p53 is mutated or missing in over half of all human cancers.

**What is signal amplification?**
Signal amplification means one signal molecule can trigger a huge cellular response. In a phosphorylation cascade, each kinase activates many of the next kinase down the chain. By the end, what started as one signal can affect thousands of downstream proteins. This is why hormones can have such dramatic effects at very low concentrations.

**Why do cells need checkpoints?**
Checkpoints prevent cells from dividing if conditions aren't right. The G1 checkpoint stops cells with damaged DNA, insufficient nutrients, or wrong size. The G2 checkpoint verifies DNA is fully replicated. The M checkpoint ensures chromosomes are properly attached before separating. Without checkpoints, cells would divide with damaged DNA, missing chromosomes, or other defects — leading to cancer or cell death.

**What happens when apoptosis fails?**
Apoptosis is the body's way of removing dangerous, damaged, or unnecessary cells. When apoptosis fails, cells that should die instead survive. This can lead to cancer (cells with damaged DNA keep dividing), autoimmune disease (immune cells that should self-destruct attack the body), or developmental problems (extra cells that should have been removed during embryonic development).

**Why do steroid hormones use intracellular receptors instead of membrane receptors?**
Steroid hormones are hydrophobic (lipid-soluble), so they can pass directly through the plasma membrane and bind to receptors inside the cell — usually in the cytoplasm or nucleus. Once bound, the receptor-hormone complex acts as a transcription factor, directly turning genes on or off. Hydrophilic signals like peptide hormones can't cross the membrane and need surface receptors instead.
