Unit 7 of AP Biology studies how populations evolve over time. ## Key concepts to master

### Darwin's theory of natural selection

Darwin's core ideas, based on his work on the *Origin of Species* (1859):

1. **Variation exists** in every population (different individuals have different traits)
2. **Some variations are heritable** (passed from parent to offspring)
3. **More offspring are produced than can survive** (competition for limited resources)
4. **Organisms with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce** (differential reproductive success)
5. **Over generations, favorable traits become more common** in the population

**Key insight:** natural selection acts on **individuals**, but populations **evolve**.

## Evidence for evolution

| Type of evidence | What it shows |
|---|---|
| **Fossil record** | Shows change over time, transitional forms (Tiktaalik, Archaeopteryx, whale ancestors) |
| **Biogeography** | Distribution of species reflects shared ancestry and geographic isolation (Galápagos finches) |
| **Comparative anatomy** | Homologous structures (similar structure, common ancestor); analogous structures (similar function, different ancestor); vestigial structures (leftover from ancestors) |
| **Comparative embryology** | Vertebrate embryos look similar early in development |
| **Molecular biology** | DNA, RNA, and protein sequences show shared ancestry across all life; the more similar the sequences, the closer the relationship |
| **Direct observation** | Antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects, peppered moths during industrial revolution |

### Homologous vs analogous structures

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Homologous** | Similar **structure**, common ancestor (may have different functions) | Forelimbs of bats, whales, humans, cats |
| **Analogous** | Similar **function**, different ancestors (convergent evolution) | Wings of birds and insects |
| **Vestigial** | Reduced or non-functional structure inherited from an ancestor | Human appendix, whale pelvic bones |

## Mechanisms of evolution (5 things that change allele frequencies)

| Mechanism | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Natural selection** | Favors individuals with traits that improve survival/reproduction |
| **Genetic drift** | Random changes in allele frequency (especially in small populations) |
| **Gene flow** | Movement of alleles between populations through migration |
| **Mutation** | Source of new alleles |
| **Non-random mating** | When individuals choose mates non-randomly (sexual selection, inbreeding) |

### Genetic drift in detail

| Type | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Bottleneck effect** | Population suddenly reduced (disaster, disease) → surviving alleles may not represent original population (e.g., cheetahs) |
| **Founder effect** | Small group leaves to start a new population → only a small subset of original alleles → may differ from source population (e.g., Amish populations) |

## Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium

A null model — describes a population that is **NOT evolving**.

### The five Hardy-Weinberg conditions

A population is in equilibrium (not evolving) only if **all five** are true:

1. **No mutations**
2. **No natural selection**
3. **No genetic drift** (very large population)
4. **No gene flow** (no migration)
5. **Random mating**

In real life, populations almost never meet all five — they're almost always evolving.

### The Hardy-Weinberg equations

**Allele frequency equation:**
**p + q = 1**

- **p** = frequency of dominant allele
- **q** = frequency of recessive allele

**Genotype frequency equation:**
**p² + 2pq + q² = 1**

- **p²** = frequency of homozygous dominant (AA)
- **2pq** = frequency of heterozygous (Aa)
- **q²** = frequency of homozygous recessive (aa)

### Hardy-Weinberg problem-solving steps

1. Start with the recessive phenotype frequency = **q²**
2. Take square root to find **q**
3. Calculate **p** = 1 − q
4. Calculate **p²** and **2pq**
5. If genotype frequencies match Hardy-Weinberg predictions, the population is in equilibrium

## Types of natural selection

| Type | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Directional selection** | Favors one extreme phenotype; shifts the population in one direction | Dark moths during the industrial revolution |
| **Stabilizing selection** | Favors the average phenotype; reduces extremes | Human birth weight |
| **Disruptive selection** | Favors both extremes, against the average | Beak size in some Galápagos finches (large for nuts, small for seeds, medium for nothing) |
| **Sexual selection** | Traits that improve mating success — even if they reduce survival | Peacock tails, deer antlers |
| **Artificial selection** | Humans select for desired traits in domesticated species | Dog breeds, crops, livestock |

---

## Coevolution and adaptive radiation

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Coevolution** | Two species evolve in response to each other (predator-prey, flowers and pollinators, parasites and hosts) |
| **Adaptive radiation** | One ancestor diversifies into many species filling different niches (Galápagos finches, Hawaiian honeycreepers) |
| **Convergent evolution** | Unrelated species evolve similar traits because they live in similar environments (sharks and dolphins) |
| **Divergent evolution** | Related species become different over time as they adapt to different environments |

## Speciation (formation of new species)

A **species** is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring (the **biological species concept**).

### Reproductive isolation

Barriers that prevent interbreeding. Speciation requires reproductive isolation.

### Prezygotic barriers (prevent fertilization)

| Barrier | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Habitat isolation** | Species live in different habitats |
| **Temporal isolation** | Mate at different times (seasons, days, times of day) |
| **Behavioral isolation** | Different courtship rituals |
| **Mechanical isolation** | Anatomical differences prevent mating |
| **Gametic isolation** | Sperm and egg can't combine even if mating occurs |

### Postzygotic barriers (prevent viable/fertile offspring)

| Barrier | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Reduced hybrid viability** | Hybrid offspring die early or are weak |
| **Reduced hybrid fertility** | Hybrid offspring are sterile (mules, ligers) |
| **Hybrid breakdown** | Hybrid offspring are healthy, but their offspring are sterile or weak |

### Two main modes of speciation

| Mode | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Allopatric speciation** | Population is geographically separated → independent evolution → new species (most common) |
| **Sympatric speciation** | New species forms without geographic separation (polyploidy in plants, niche differentiation) |

## Patterns of evolution

| Pattern | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Gradualism** | Evolution happens slowly and continuously over long time |
| **Punctuated equilibrium** | Long periods of little change interrupted by short bursts of rapid change (often after environmental change) |

The fossil record supports both patterns in different lineages.

## Phylogenetic trees and cladograms

Diagrams showing evolutionary relationships among species.

### Reading a phylogenetic tree

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Node** | A point where lineages split (a common ancestor) |
| **Branch** | A line representing a lineage over time |
| **Tip / leaf** | The end of a branch (a current species) |
| **Common ancestor** | The most recent shared ancestor of two species |
| **Clade** | A group that includes a common ancestor and **all** of its descendants |
| **Sister taxa** | Two species that share an immediate common ancestor |

### Key principles

- The **closer two species are on the tree**, the more recently they shared a common ancestor
- **Branch length** can represent time or amount of genetic change (depends on the tree)
- A tree shows **relationships**, not "advanced" vs "primitive" species
- Modern species are at the **tips**, not in the middle of the tree

### Building trees

Trees are built using:
- **Morphological data** (anatomy, structure)
- **Molecular data** (DNA, RNA, protein sequences) — most accurate today
- **Shared derived characters** (synapomorphies)
- **Parsimony** — the simplest tree (fewest evolutionary changes) is preferred

## Extinction

| Type | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Background extinction** | Constant low rate of extinction throughout history |
| **Mass extinction** | Catastrophic event wiping out many species at once |

### The 5 major mass extinctions

1. **Ordovician–Silurian** (~440 million years ago) — climate change, glaciation
2. **Late Devonian** (~370 mya) — possibly anoxic oceans
3. **Permian–Triassic** (~252 mya) — the **biggest** ever, ~96% of marine species lost
4. **Triassic–Jurassic** (~200 mya) — likely volcanic activity
5. **Cretaceous–Paleogene** (~66 mya) — asteroid impact, killed non-avian dinosaurs

**Some scientists argue we're in a 6th mass extinction** caused by human activity (habitat destruction, climate change, pollution).

## Origin of life

How life began on Earth — still a major open question, but key ideas include:

| Concept | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| **Primordial soup hypothesis** | Early Earth had organic molecules in oceans powered by lightning, UV, heat (Miller-Urey experiment showed amino acids could form abiotically) |
| **RNA world hypothesis** | RNA, not DNA, was the first genetic material — RNA can store information AND act as a catalyst (ribozymes) |
| **Endosymbiotic theory** | Eukaryotic cells evolved when a larger cell engulfed a prokaryote (mitochondria, chloroplasts) — Unit 2 connection |
| **Earliest fossils** | ~3.5 billion years old (stromatolites, formed by cyanobacteria) |

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Saying "individuals evolve"** — individuals **don't** evolve; **populations** evolve
- **Saying "evolution has a goal"** — evolution doesn't have a direction or purpose; it just happens based on environmental pressures
- **Confusing homologous and analogous** — homologous = common ancestor; analogous = similar function only
- **Forgetting Hardy-Weinberg conditions** — populations rarely meet all five (so they're usually evolving)
- **Mixing up bottleneck and founder effect** — bottleneck = population reduced; founder = small group leaves and starts a new population
- **Confusing types of selection** — directional shifts the average; stabilizing favors the average; disruptive favors both extremes
- **Saying "humans evolved from monkeys"** — humans and monkeys share a common ancestor; monkeys still exist
- **Misreading phylogenetic trees** — closeness on the tree (shared common ancestor) matters more than appearance similarity

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 7?**
Unit 7 makes up about 13–20% of multiple-choice questions — the highest-weighted unit on the entire exam. Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, natural selection, speciation, and phylogenetic trees are the most-tested concepts. If you have to prioritize one unit, prioritize this one.

**What's the difference between natural selection and evolution?**
Natural selection is a **mechanism** of evolution — one of several ways allele frequencies change. Evolution is the broader process of change in allele frequencies over time. Genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, and non-random mating are also mechanisms of evolution. Natural selection is the most famous, but it's not the only one.

**What's the difference between homologous and analogous structures?**
Homologous structures share a common ancestor — they have similar underlying anatomy even if they look or function differently (a bat's wing, a whale's flipper, and a human's arm all have the same bones). Analogous structures evolved separately and look similar because they perform similar functions (a bird's wing and a butterfly's wing). Homology supports common descent; analogy doesn't.

**Why is sexual selection a thing if it sometimes hurts survival?**
Traits that improve mating success can spread even if they reduce survival, because evolution is about **reproductive success**, not just survival. A peacock's tail makes it more visible to predators, but it also helps attract mates. As long as the reproductive benefit outweighs the survival cost, the trait spreads. This is why sexual selection often produces extreme traits.

**How do you tell if a population is evolving?**
Compare the population's allele frequencies to those predicted by Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. If the frequencies don't match, the population is evolving. Real populations almost never match exactly because they don't meet all five Hardy-Weinberg conditions (no mutation, no selection, no drift, no gene flow, random mating).

**Why is the Galápagos finch story important?**
Darwin observed that the finches on different Galápagos islands had different beak shapes adapted to different food sources (seeds, insects, cactus). All the finches descended from a single ancestor that arrived on the islands long ago. As the population spread across the islands, geographic isolation and different selection pressures (different food sources) led to **adaptive radiation** — one ancestor diversifying into many species filling different niches.

**What is a clade?**
A clade is a group of organisms that includes a common ancestor and **all** of its descendants. Mammals are a clade. Reptiles, in the traditional sense, are **not** a clade because they don't include all descendants — birds descend from reptiles but are usually grouped separately. Cladistics organizes life based on shared evolutionary history rather than appearance.

**Are humans causing a 6th mass extinction?**
Many biologists argue yes. Current extinction rates are estimated to be 100–1,000 times higher than the natural background rate, and the causes are linked to human activity — habitat destruction, climate change, invasive species, pollution, and direct overexploitation. Whether this qualifies as a "mass extinction" depends on definition, but the rate of biodiversity loss is unprecedented in the geological short term.
