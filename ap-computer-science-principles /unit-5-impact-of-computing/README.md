## What is Unit 5 about?

Unit 5 of AP Computer Science Principles studies the social, ethical, and personal effects of computing. You'll learn about the beneficial and harmful effects of technology, the digital divide, computing bias, citizen science, intellectual property, creative commons licensing, cybersecurity threats, encryption, and how to protect personally identifiable information (PII). This unit makes up about 21–26% of the exam — the **second-biggest unit** behind Unit 3 — and is mostly **conceptual reading and reasoning**, not coding. Strong vocabulary and the ability to apply concepts to real-world scenarios are critical here.

## Key concepts to master

### Beneficial and harmful effects of computing

Almost every computing technology has both **positive** and **negative** consequences. The exam often asks you to identify both sides of a technology.

| Technology | Beneficial effects | Harmful effects |
|---|---|---|
| **Social media** | Connects people, spreads information, supports communities | Spreads misinformation, harms mental health, enables harassment |
| **Smartphones** | Access to information, communication, productivity | Addiction, distraction, surveillance |
| **Self-driving cars** | Could reduce accidents, more accessible transport | Job losses (drivers), liability questions, security risks |
| **Online shopping** | Convenience, lower prices, wider selection | Job losses (retail), packaging waste, data collection |
| **AI and automation** | Increases productivity, solves complex problems | Job displacement, bias, accountability issues |

**Key idea for the exam:** technology is rarely all good or all bad. Be ready to discuss **trade-offs**.

## The digital divide

The **digital divide** is the gap between people who have access to modern computing and those who don't.

### Causes of the digital divide

| Cause | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Income** | Lower-income households can't afford devices or internet |
| **Geography** | Rural areas have less broadband infrastructure |
| **Age** | Older adults may have less access or familiarity |
| **Education** | Schools in poorer areas have fewer tech resources |
| **Disability** | Software may not be accessible to all users |
| **Country / region** | Developing nations have less infrastructure |

### Why it matters

- Lack of access limits **education**, **employment**, **healthcare**, and **civic participation**
- Widening gap between those connected and those not
- Unequal benefits from new technology
- Solutions include public Wi-Fi, low-cost devices, digital literacy programs

## Computing bias

Computer programs are not neutral — they reflect the **values and assumptions** of the people who created them and the **data** they were trained on.

### How bias shows up in computing

| Source | Example |
|---|---|
| **Biased training data** | Facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces — fails on darker skin tones |
| **Biased developers** | A team that lacks diversity may not anticipate certain users' needs |
| **Biased assumptions in algorithms** | Loan approval AI penalizes neighborhoods historically denied loans |
| **Sampling bias** | Surveying only certain demographics misses others |

### Real examples

- **Amazon's hiring algorithm** discriminated against women because it was trained on resumes that mostly came from men
- **Facial recognition systems** with high error rates for women and people of color
- **Predictive policing software** sending more police to already over-policed neighborhoods
- **Search engines** displaying biased autocomplete suggestions

### Why bias is hard to fix

- Bias often hides in seemingly neutral data
- Removing bias requires diverse teams and intentional testing
- Even well-intentioned developers can produce biased systems

## Crowdsourcing and citizen science

### Crowdsourcing

Asking a large group of people for help — usually online — to complete a task.

| Type | Example |
|---|---|
| **Knowledge crowdsourcing** | Wikipedia, Stack Overflow |
| **Funding crowdsourcing** | Kickstarter, GoFundMe |
| **Labor crowdsourcing** | Amazon Mechanical Turk |
| **Innovation crowdsourcing** | Open-source software, hackathons |

### Citizen science

Members of the public help collect or process scientific data.

**Examples:**
- **eBird** — birdwatchers report sightings to track migration
- **Foldit** — gamers help fold proteins for medical research
- **Galaxy Zoo** — volunteers classify galaxies in telescope images
- **iNaturalist** — users identify plants and animals worldwide

### Benefits

- Massive amounts of data collected quickly
- Public engagement with science
- Often free or low-cost
- Solutions can come from unexpected places

### Drawbacks

- Quality control can be hard
- Privacy concerns for participants
- Volunteer labor not always compensated

## Intellectual property and creative commons

### Intellectual property

Legal protections for ideas and creative works.

| Type | What it protects | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Copyright** | Creative works (books, music, software, photos) | Movies, songs, novels |
| **Patent** | Inventions and unique processes | Drug formulas, mechanical inventions |
| **Trademark** | Brand names, logos, slogans | Nike swoosh, McDonald's golden arches |

### Plagiarism vs piracy

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Plagiarism** | Presenting someone else's work as your own |
| **Piracy** | Copying or distributing copyrighted material without permission |

### Creative Commons

A set of licenses that let creators share their work while controlling how others use it.

| License element | What it requires |
|---|---|
| **BY** (Attribution) | Credit the creator |
| **NC** (Non-Commercial) | No commercial use |
| **ND** (No Derivatives) | No modifications allowed |
| **SA** (Share Alike) | Modifications must use the same license |

### Open source software

Software whose source code is available for anyone to view, modify, and distribute. Examples: Linux, Firefox, Python, VS Code.

### Why this matters for the Create Task

The Create Performance Task **requires** that any code or assets you didn't create yourself must be properly attributed. Using copyrighted material without permission or credit can disqualify your task.

## Cybersecurity

The practice of protecting digital systems from attacks, theft, and damage.

### Common cybersecurity threats

| Threat | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Malware** | Malicious software (viruses, worms, trojans, spyware) |
| **Phishing** | Tricking users into revealing passwords or info via fake emails or sites |
| **Ransomware** | Encrypts files and demands payment to unlock |
| **DDoS attack** | Floods a server with traffic to take it offline |
| **Keylogger** | Records keystrokes to steal passwords |
| **Man-in-the-middle attack** | Intercepts communication between two parties |
| **SQL injection** | Inserts malicious code into a website's database query |
| **Brute force attack** | Tries many passwords until one works |

### Phishing in detail

A common social engineering attack where an attacker pretends to be a trusted source.

**Common signs:**
- Urgent or threatening language ("Your account will be closed!")
- Suspicious sender address
- Misspelled words or unusual formatting
- Links that don't match the claimed website
- Requests for personal info or passwords

## Authentication and protection

### Multi-factor authentication (MFA / 2FA)

Combines two or more factors to verify identity:

| Factor | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Something you know** | Password, PIN |
| **Something you have** | Phone, security key |
| **Something you are** | Fingerprint, face scan |

### Strong passwords

| Best practice | Why |
|---|---|
| **Long** (12+ characters) | Harder to brute-force |
| **Mix of types** (upper, lower, digits, symbols) | More combinations to try |
| **Unique per account** | One leak doesn't compromise everything |
| **Use a password manager** | Lets you have unique strong passwords without memorizing |

## Encryption

Encoding data so only authorized parties can read it.

### Two main types of encryption

| Type | Key structure | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Symmetric encryption** | Same key for encryption and decryption | Encrypting files at rest |
| **Asymmetric encryption (public key)** | Public key encrypts; private key decrypts | HTTPS, secure messaging |

### How HTTPS works (simplified)

1. Your browser asks the website for its public key
2. Your browser encrypts data with that public key
3. Only the website's private key can decrypt it
4. Anyone intercepting the traffic sees only encrypted data

### Why encryption matters

- Protects passwords, credit card numbers, personal messages
- Required for online banking, healthcare, and any sensitive data
- HTTPS (the lock icon in browsers) signals encrypted communication

## Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

Data that can identify a specific person.

### Examples of PII

- **Direct PII** — full name, Social Security number, driver's license, passport
- **Indirect PII** — date of birth, ZIP code, IP address (combined, can identify someone)
- **Sensitive PII** — medical records, financial info, biometric data

### Why protecting PII matters

- **Identity theft** — using someone's PII to impersonate them
- **Financial fraud** — stealing money or opening accounts in your name
- **Stalking and harassment** — using info to find or harm someone
- **Discrimination** — using info to deny opportunities

### How to protect PII

- Limit what you share online
- Use strong passwords and 2FA
- Check privacy settings on apps and websites
- Watch for phishing
- Use HTTPS sites for transactions
- Be cautious with public Wi-Fi

## Digital footprint

Everything you do online leaves a trace — your **digital footprint**.

### Types of digital footprint

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Active** | Things you intentionally post (social media, comments, reviews) |
| **Passive** | Things tracked without you noticing (browsing history, location, cookies) |

### Why it matters

- **Permanence** — once posted, even "deleted" content can persist
- **Employers and colleges** check social media
- **Advertisers** build profiles based on behavior
- **Hackers and scammers** use this data
- Hard to fully erase

## Common AP exam mistakes

- **Treating computing as all good or all bad** — be ready to identify trade-offs
- **Confusing crowdsourcing and citizen science** — citizen science is specifically scientific data
- **Mixing up plagiarism and piracy** — plagiarism = claiming credit; piracy = copying without permission
- **Forgetting that bias hides in data** — even neutral-seeming algorithms reflect their training data
- **Saying "encryption is unbreakable"** — strong encryption is hard to break, but no system is 100% secure
- **Ignoring the digital divide in policy questions** — exam often tests awareness of access inequality
- **Confusing symmetric and asymmetric encryption** — symmetric uses one key; asymmetric uses two
- **Assuming PII is just names and SSNs** — IP address, ZIP code, and birth date can identify someone when combined

## Frequently asked

**How much of the AP exam is Unit 5?**
Unit 5 makes up about 21–26% of multiple-choice questions — the **second-largest unit** behind Unit 3. Together, Units 3 and 5 cover over half the exam. If you skip Unit 5, you can't pass.

**What's the digital divide and why is it a problem?**
The digital divide is the gap between people who have access to modern computing — devices, fast internet, digital literacy — and those who don't. It matters because so much of modern life now requires digital access: education, jobs, healthcare, banking, government services. People without access fall behind in every way and the gap widens over time.

**What's the difference between phishing and malware?**
Phishing is a **social engineering** attack — it tricks the user into voluntarily giving up information (usually through fake emails or websites). Malware is **malicious software** that runs on a computer to cause harm (viruses, ransomware, spyware). Phishing often delivers malware: a phishing email tricks you into opening an attachment that installs malware.

**What's the difference between symmetric and asymmetric encryption?**
Symmetric encryption uses **one key** to both encrypt and decrypt data — fast and good for large files, but the key must be shared securely. Asymmetric encryption uses **two keys**: a public key (anyone can use it to encrypt) and a private key (only the owner has it, and only it can decrypt). HTTPS uses asymmetric encryption to securely set up a session, then often switches to symmetric for speed.

**Why does the Create Task care about citing sources?**
The Create Performance Task is a real assessment of your programming work. Using someone else's code or assets without permission or credit is **plagiarism** — and the College Board can disqualify your submission. Always cite borrowed code, follow the licenses of any libraries you use, and use Creative Commons or public domain assets when needed.

**What's a digital footprint?**
A digital footprint is the trail of data you leave online — everything you post, search for, like, click, and browse. Active footprint is what you intentionally share; passive footprint is what gets tracked behind the scenes (cookies, location, browsing history). Your digital footprint is mostly permanent and harder to erase than people think.

**Why does computing bias matter?**
Computers are increasingly used for high-stakes decisions — hiring, loans, criminal justice, healthcare. If the algorithms behind these decisions have bias built in (from biased data, biased developers, or flawed assumptions), they can systematically discriminate against certain groups. Bias in computing can amplify and spread inequality at scale, much faster than human bias alone.

**What's the difference between copyright and patent?**
Copyright protects **creative works** — books, music, software, photos. It's automatic when you create something. Patents protect **inventions and unique processes** — like drug formulas or mechanical designs. Patents must be applied for and granted by a patent office, and they expire after a set time (usually 20 years).

**Why is multi-factor authentication better than just a password?**
Passwords can be stolen through phishing, leaks, or guessing. With MFA, even if someone has your password, they still need a second factor (a code on your phone, a fingerprint, a hardware key) to log in. This dramatically reduces the chance of an account being compromised — even if your password is leaked.
